## Abstract 

Vision-language models transfer well in zero-shot settings, but at deployment the visual and textual branches often shift asymmet-rically. Under this condition, entropy-based test-time adaptation can sharpen the fused posterior while increasing error, because an unreliable modality may still dominate fusion. We study this failure mode through a majorization view of multimodal posteriors and cast adaptation as a constrained de-mixing problem on the fused prediction. Based on this view, we propose MG-MTTA, which keeps the backbone frozen and updates only a lightweight gate or adapter. The objective combines fused-posterior entropy minimization with a reliability-aware gate prior built from anchor-based modality con-sistency and cross-modal conflict. Our analysis gives conditions under which entropy reduction preserves the correct ranking and a threshold that characterizes modality-dominance failure. On the ImageNet-based benchmark, MG-MTTA improves top-1 accuracy from 57.97 to 66.51 under semantics-preserving textual shift and from 21.68 to 26.27 under joint visual-textual shift, while remaining competitive in the visual-only benchmark. These results show that multimodal test-time adaptation should control modality reliability, not just prediction entropy.

## 1 Introduction 

Vision-language models (VLMs) have become a standard founda-tion for multimodal recognition, retrieval, and open-vocabulary understanding. Their large-scale pretraining substantially improves zero-shot transfer and cross-modal reasoning across diverse tasks. However, realistic deployment rarely preserves the source-domain conditions seen during pretraining [ 3, 14 ]. Instead, the two modal-ities are often perturbed asymmetrically: the visual stream may suffer from blur, occlusion, low illumination, or style shift, while the textual stream may exhibit prompt-template drift, lexical variation, or other forms of semantics-preserving phrasing shift [ 3 , 21 , 29 ]. The resulting challenge is therefore not merely uncertainty under a single distribution shift, but multimodal decision-making under modality-specific shift. This asymmetry creates a failure mode that is specific to multi-modal fusion. When one modality becomes unreliable but remains artificially sharp, entropy minimization can further increase confi-dence in an incorrect fused decision [ 25 ]. In other words, a lower-entropy posterior is not necessarily a better posterior once fusion is driven by modality imbalance. A satisfactory test-time adaptation method for VLMs should therefore do more than sharpen predic-tions: it should identify when entropy reduction corresponds to genuine recovery and when it merely amplifies the influence of a corrupted modality. Our starting point is to view modality-specific shift through pos-terior geometry. We model each modality-level posterior as a doubly stochastic mixing of an underlying clean posterior, up to a residual that captures deviation from the idealized mixing model [ 10 ]. Under this view, test-time adaptation becomes a constrained de-mixing problem on the fused posterior. This perspective is useful for two reasons. First, it provides a principled explanation of why entropy may increase under shift and why a successful update should re-verse that trend only along a valid majorization direction [ 10 , 17 ]. Second, it exposes the role of modality reliability in fusion: if one stream is poorly explained by the mixing model or conflicts sharply with the other stream, its contribution should be downweighted during adaptation [ 15 , 28 ]. Throughout the paper, we distinguish between an ideal analysis object based on doubly stochastic mixing and its practical implementation. The former clarifies the majoriza-tion geometry of multimodal degradation and recovery, whereas the latter is realized by a computable modality-to-anchor reliability proxy built from running anchors. Based on this formulation, we propose a majorization-guided test-time adaptation method that keeps the vision-language back-bone frozen and updates only a lightweight gate or adapter module. The method combines entropy minimization on the fused posterior with reliability-aware regularization derived from two computable signals: an anchor-based reliability proxy inspired by doubly sto-chastic fit, and a cross-modality disagreement proxy that reflects conflict between the two streams. The resulting objective keeps the trainable subset small and is directly aligned with the theoretical quantities used in our analysis. Our contributions are summarized as follows. 

• We identify a multimodal-specific failure mode of entropy-based test-time adaptation under modality-specific shift, where lower entropy can reflect confidence amplification by a biased modality rather than genuine recovery. 

• We formulate this problem as a multimodal posterior de-mixing problem based on majorization and doubly stochas-tic mixing, and derive conditions under which entropy re-duction is beneficial together with a modality-dominance threshold that characterizes a concrete failure regime. 

• We propose a lightweight adaptation method that updates only a gate or adapter module while keeping the pretrained backbone frozen, using reliability-aware regularization based on an anchor-based reliability proxy and cross-modality disagreement. 

• We establish an evaluation protocol that separates visual shift, semantics-preserving textual shift, and joint shift, while treating conflicting textual perturbations as failure analysis rather than as part of the main robustness bench-mark. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Test-Time Adaptation 

Test-time adaptation (TTA) aims to adapt a source model to un-labeled target data at inference time without full retraining [ 7 ]. Representative methods reduce predictive entropy, update normal-ization statistics, stabilize predictions with teacher models, filter unreliable samples, or restrict adaptation to small parameter sub-sets for robustness [ 11 , 12 , 22 , 23 ]. These approaches have been especially effective in image classification, where the model output is usually treated as a single posterior to be sharpened or stabilized under shift. The multimodal case is more delicate. Once prediction depends on both visual and textual streams, lower entropy does not by itself imply a better decision. The outcome also depends on whether fusion remains aligned with the more reliable modality. This dif-ference motivates the setting considered here, where the visual posterior, the textual posterior, and the fused posterior are treated as separate but coupled objects. ## 2.2 Vision-Language Models under Shift and Test-Time Adaptation 

Vision-language models built on contrastive pretraining and large-scale multimodal learning have substantially improved transfer across recognition, retrieval, and open-vocabulary tasks [ 1 , 16 , 26 ]. At the same time, their predictions remain sensitive to prompt de-sign, visual corruptions, and broader distribution shift [ 4, 13 , 24 ]. Existing studies have mainly documented this sensitivity through performance degradation, calibration, or prompt robustness anal-yses [ 3, 14 ], making clear that robustness remains fragile once deployment conditions depart from pretraining. Recent work has therefore extended TTA to vision-language and multimodal models through prompt tuning, lightweight adaptation, or reliability-aware correction [ 5, 9, 18 , 25 ]. These methods are the closest practical neighbors to our setting because they adapt frozen or lightly updated multimodal backbones under target shift. Still, most existing formulations are organized around entropy min-imization, consistency, or heuristic reliability adjustment. Although robustness is evaluated on the fused prediction, the fused posterior itself is usually not treated as the object of analysis. As a result, current methods say relatively little about when entropy reduction preserves the correct fused ranking and when it instead reinforces a biased modality. Our formulation focuses on this point directly by studying multimodal adaptation at the fused-posterior level. 

## 2.3 Fusion, Reliability, and Majorization 

A separate line of work studies multimodal fusion under modal-ity imbalance and shows that reliability-aware weighting can im-prove prediction by reducing the influence of noisy or misaligned streams [ 6 , 20 , 27 , 28 ]. This line of research provides an impor-tant practical intuition for our setting: when the modalities are not equally trustworthy, robust fusion should not weight them equally. Separately, majorization theory provides a natural language for describing how probability vectors become more mixed or less mixed [ 10 ]. While it is closely related to entropy and posterior geometry, it has rarely been used to study multimodal adaptation. In this paper, majorization is not used only as a descriptive tool. It is also used to clarify when entropy reduction corresponds to genuine recovery and when a sharper prediction is simply a sharper mistake under modality-specific shift. 

## 3 Method 

MG-MTTA adapts a frozen vision-language model by updating only a lightweight fusion module at test time. For each test sample, the model first produces visual and textual predictions, then estimates modality reliability from running anchors and cross-modality con-flict, and finally regularizes the fusion gate toward the more reliable modality. The fused prediction is optimized with entropy minimiza-tion, gate regularization, and batch diversity. The rest of this section first states the practical adaptation rule used in deployment and then introduces the majorization-based mixing view that motivates it.

## 4 Experiments 4.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate MG-MTTA under three test regimes: visual shift, semantics-preserving textual shift, and joint shift. The visual setting serves to anchor the method to standard corruption benchmarks, while the textual and joint settings more directly test the multimodal fusion failures that motivate this paper. We further use diagnostic analy-ses and failure cases to examine whether the observed behavior is consistent with the proposed majorization-based view. 

4.1.1 Benchmarks and shift construction. All main-text experiments are instantiated under a shared ImageNet label space, which allows visual, textual, and joint shift to be compared within a single proto-col. Visual robustness is evaluated on CIFAR-100C and ImageNet-C from the common-corruptions benchmark family [ 4]. Unless other-wise stated, we report mean top-1 accuracy over the 15 corruption types at severity level 5. This part mainly serves as a benchmark-anchoring comparison to existing CLIP test-time adaptation results. The textual setting is constructed differently. We keep the image fixed and perturb only the prompt bank, while preserving class iden-tity. The L1–L4 ladder is not built from arbitrary prompt rewrites. Instead, it is motivated by the input-processing mechanism of CLIP-style text encoders, whose representations are sensitive to token boundaries, character-level perturbations, and local prompt con-text. We therefore apply label-preserving token- and context-level perturbations that progressively destabilize the text representation without changing the underlying class semantics. The resulting perturbations include prompt-template drift, lexical substitution, and removal of non-essential modifiers, and are averaged into a unified textual-shift score over the same ImageNet label space. Joint shift combines image corruption with the semantics-preserving textual perturbations above. By contrast, prompt perturbations Lixian Chen, Mingxuan Huang, Yanhui Chen, Junyi Lin, and Yang Shi 

that conflict with class semantics—such as negation, incorrect at-tributes, or class-inconsistent descriptions—are excluded from the main benchmark and reserved for failure analysis, because they probe the modality-dominance mechanism in Sec. 3.5 more directly than the main robustness protocol does. This separation keeps the visual benchmark comparable to prior work, while using the textual and joint settings to test whether adaptation can recover reliable multimodal fusion under asymmetric shift. Full perturbation rules and additional protocol details are deferred to Appendix F. 

4.1.2 Compared methods. Our comparisons are organized around two baseline groups. The main comparisons in the textual and joint settings use matched internal baselines, so that backbone, prompt bank, update scope, and adaptation budget remain fixed across methods. In this group, Source-only evaluates the frozen vision-language backbone without test-time adaptation. Entropy-only up-dates the same parameter subset as MG-MTTA but optimizes only fused-posterior entropy. Entropy + Diversity adds the diversity reg-ularizer on top of fused entropy minimization. These baselines are compared against MG-MTTA , which uses fused entropy minimiza-tion together with proxy-guided gate regularization and diversity regularization. We also report representative external CLIP test-time adaptation baselines in the visual-shift setting, including TENT-Visual [ 22 ], SAR [ 12 ], TPT [ 19 ], VTE [ 2 ], and BATCLIP [ 9]. These methods were primarily introduced for visual corruption or prompt adaptation benchmarks, so we use them only in the visual comparison. For textual and joint shift, we restrict the comparison to the matched in-ternal baselines in order to keep the multimodal evaluation protocol controlled and directly comparable. 

4.1.3 Implementation and fairness protocol. All main-text exper-iments use CLIP ViT-B/16 as the primary vision-language back-bone [ 16 ]. Unless noted otherwise, both modality encoders are frozen. ## 5 Limitations 

MG-MTTA is designed for modality-specific shift, especially textual and joint regimes where asymmetric fusion errors dominate. On visual-only corruption benchmarks, it is competitive with matched entropy-based variants but not the strongest external CLIP adapta-tion baseline. The method relies on running anchors and a calibrated semantics-preserving textual stress protocol. Runtime and memory overhead are not separately quantified in this version, so efficiency claims are limited to the small trainable parameter subset. 

## 6 Conclusion 

This paper studies test-time adaptation for vision-language models under modality-specific shift. We formulate multimodal adapta-tion as a de-mixing problem on the fused posterior and show that entropy reduction alone is insufficient: it is useful only when the adapted posterior follows a valid majorization-consistent direction while preserving the decisive ranking. MG-MTTA implements this view by keeping the CLIP ViT-B/16 backbone frozen and adapting a lightweight gate or adapter with fused-posterior entropy mini-mization, reliability-aware gate regularization, and batch diversity. The diagnostics and failure cases are consistent with the proposed explanation: entropy-only adaptation may sharpen the wrong fused decision, whereas reliability-aware adaptation more often aligns Majorization-Guided Test-Time Adaptation for Vision-Language Models under Modality-Specific Shift 

entropy reduction with recovery. The main empirical advantage ap-pears in textual and joint regimes, where asymmetric perturbations make multimodal fusion the core test-time adaptation challenge.

## H Prompt Construction and Textual Shift Protocol 

Our textual-shift design is guided by the text-processing mechanism of CLIP-style vision–language models rather than by arbitrary prompt rewriting. In CLIP/OpenCLIP-style models, the text branch maps each prompt into a fixed-length token sequence and then into a single text representation used for image–text matching. This makes the model sensitive not only to prompt semantics, but also to token boundaries, character-level perturbations, and local prompt context. Motivated by this property, we construct a token-level textual stress protocol that perturbs the prompt in ways that remain largely label-preserving while progressively degrading the stability of the text representation. The textual-shift protocol is divided into two categories. 

Semantics-preserving textual stress. This category is used in the main experiments. Its goal is not to change the class label, but to perturb the prompt at the token and local-context level in a con-trolled manner. Compared with purely semantic paraphrasing, this construction is more directly tied to how CLIP-style text encoders tokenize and represent prompts. Accordingly, the main protocol operates on token boundaries, character-level spelling noise, and mild context degradation while preserving the underlying class identity. We instantiate a five-level token-level textual stress ladder: 

• L1: context-only typo. Mild character-level perturbation is applied to prompt-context words such as photo , image ,or picture , without modifying the class phrase itself. 

• L2: context typo + class-boundary perturbation. In addition to the L1 context perturbation, the class phrase is modified at the boundary level, for example by replacing spaces with hyphens or underscores, while avoiding inner-token corruption. 

• L3: partial mild class-token corruption. Based on L2, a deterministic subset of classes receives an additional mild inner-token perturbation on the class phrase. This increases difficulty while retaining approximate human readability. 

• L4: expanded class-token corruption with mild con-text drop. Relative to L3, the subset of classes receiving class-token corruption is enlarged, and the local grammat-ical context is mildly degraded. This produces a stronger but still label-preserving prompt shift. Majorization-Guided Test-Time Adaptation for Vision-Language Models under Modality-Specific Shift

Reason for the separation. The main comparison evaluates adapta-tion under controlled, label-preserving textual stress rather than un-der explicit semantic contradiction. Separating semantics-preserving stress from conflicting textual perturbations prevents the main benchmark from being confounded by prompts that directly alter the decision semantics. This separation also matches the theoretical narrative of the paper: the main protocol probes robustness under modality-specific but still semantically aligned shift, whereas the conflicting protocol is used to expose concrete failure regimes. 

## I Additional Metrics and Reliability Proxies 

In addition to task accuracy, we record the following theory-aligned auxiliary measurements. 

Entropy change. For each sample, we record the entropy of the pre-adaptation fused posterior and the entropy of the adapted fused posterior. This quantity verifies that the deployed method is indeed performing entropy reduction, but it is not treated as sufficient evidence of successful adaptation on its own. 

Majorization ratio. For each shift setting, we compute the pro-portion of samples for which the adapted fused posterior majorizes the pre-adaptation fused posterior, as well as the proportion of samples for which the adapted fused posterior moves closer to a clean proxy posterior in sorted partial sums. This metric is the most direct operational counterpart of the majorization-based de-mixing view in the main text.

Cross-modality conflict. We record the Jensen–Shannon diver-gence term, the ranking disagreement term, and the combined conflict score used in the gate prior. These quantities character-ize how strongly the visual and textual branches disagree under modality-specific shift. 

Collapse statistics. At the batch level, we record the entropy of the batch marginal, class-frequency imbalance, and additional col-lapse indicators used in the implementation. These measurements are used to detect whether entropy reduction is accompanied by undesirable batch-level concentration.

This calibration is included to verify that the textual-stress ladder is monotonic and severity-ordered before any test-time adap-tation is applied. In other words, the source-only calibration isolates the strength of the input-side textual perturbation itself, rather than conflating it with the behavior of a particular adaptation rule.
