Abstract 

The reliability of VLM-as-a-Judge is critical for the automatic evaluation of vision-language models (VLMs). Despite recent progress, our analysis reveals that VLM-as-a-Judge often pays limited attention to the image when mak-ing decisions. Instead, they often blindly fa-vor the more informative answer, even when they can recognize it conflicts with the image content. We call this problem informativeness bias , which significantly undermines judge re-liability. To address it, we propose BIRCH (Balanced Informativeness and Co Rrectness with a Truthful An CH or), a judging paradigm that first corrects inconsistencies with the im-age content in candidate answers, and then com-pares the answers against this corrected version. This shifts the judge’s focus from informative-ness to image-grounded correctness. Exper-iments on multiple models and benchmarks show that BIRCH reduces informativeness bias by up to 17%, resulting in performance gains of up to 9.8%. Our work reveals an overlooked but fundamental flaw in current VLM-as-a-Judge systems and highlights the need for more prin-cipled designs.

They often favor the more informative answer de-spite clear inconsistencies with the image, even when they can recognize those inconsistencies (Sec-tion 4.2). For example, in Figure 2 (A), Answer A is more truthful since it avoids a false claim about the park’s name, but is less informative. Answer B is more informative by providing a name, though it is wrong. This shows that greater informativeness can increase the risk of speculation or error (Lin et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2025). However, VLM-as-a-Judge still incorrectly selects Answer B (Figure 2 (C)), despite knowing the image gives no clue about the park’s name (Figure 2 (D)). As a result, VLM-as-a-Judge performs much worse when the more informative answer is wrong than when it happens to be truthful, as shown in Figure 1. In Section 4.4, we further show that informa-tiveness bias is much stronger than the commonly discussed length bias (Zheng et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2025) and remains significant even after controlling for length. This suggests that, although more infor-mative answers tend to be longer, the two biases are not equivalent. Consequently, prior strategies for reducing length bias, such as instructing models to ignore length (Zheng et al., 2023) or equalizing response lengths (Hu et al., 2025), are insufficient, since informativeness bias cannot be resolved by addressing length effects alone. In summary, informativeness bias is a key fac-tor that pulls VLM-as-a-Judge away from image content, undermining reliability. As a result, their judging ability lags behind their vision-language understanding, leaving much of that capability un-derused. To bridge this gap and make VLMs more reliable for evaluation, we propose a novel judg-ing paradigm BIRCH (Balanced Informativeness and Co Rrectness with a Truthful An CH or). The core idea is for the judge to first create a truthful "anchor" that encodes the necessary image content into text. Candidate answers are then compared against this anchor, bringing the judge’s attention back to the image and reducing informativeness bias. To avoid over-prioritizing correctness and unfairly rejecting valid details, the anchor is made as informative as the candidates. We achieve this by asking the judge to revise the candidate answers using the query and image, removing unsupported claims with explanations and fixing inconsistencies. The resulting anchor is both accurate and informa-tive, allowing every detail in the candidates to be verified against it, as described in Section 5. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of 8 state-of-the-art judging models, covering both open-source and commercial systems, on two benchmarks. BIRCH consistently achieves better performance than all baselines. We also confirm that BIRCH substantially reduces informativeness bias. As shown in Figure 1, it greatly improves accuracy when the more informative answer is un-truthful, demonstrating a stronger focus on image consistency, which drives the performance gains. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 1. We identify informativeness bias , where VLM-as-a-Judge is distracted by the informa-tiveness of answers and pays little attention to image content. This leaves much of its vision-language capability underused for judgment tasks, thereby undermining reliability (Section 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3). 2. We show that informativeness bias is distinct from and cannot be reduced to the commonly discussed length bias (Section 4.4), and thus calls for a solution that goes beyond simply removing length effects. 3. We propose BIRCH, a novel judging paradigm that mitigates informativeness bias and refo-cuses the judge’s attention on the image, en-abling better use of VLM capabilities and im-proving overall performance (Section 5). 

2 Related Work 

2.1 LLM/VLM-as-a-Judge Benchmarks 

Developing effective LLM/VLM-as-a-Judge sys-tems is essential for evaluating and aligning LLMs and VLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Grattafiori et al., 2024). Existing benchmarks such as Anthropic Helpful and Harmless (Bai et al., 2022), OpenAI Summarization (Stiennon et al., 2020), Reward-Bench (Lambert et al., 2025), and JudgeBench (Tan et al., 2025) have significantly advanced evaluation in the text modality (LLMs). To extend this progress to the multimodal do-main, MLLM-as-a-Judge (Chen et al., 2024a) takes the first step in quantifying VLMs’ performance as judges. VL-RewardBench (Li et al., 2025b) targets scenarios where even state-of-the-art models strug-gle. Multimodal RewardBench (Yasunaga et al., 2025) incorporates expert knowledge domains and safety-related concerns. Judge Anything (Pu et al., 2025) expands this scope to multimodal genera-tion tasks, incorporating both video and audio in-puts. MM-RLHF (Zhang et al., 2025b) improves size, diversity, annotation granularity, and includes safety-related tasks. However, despite this progress, existing works still lack a critical examination of the reliability of VLM-as-a-Judge behavior. 

2.2 Biases in LLM/VLM-as-a-Judge 

Recent work has found several cognitive biases that affect how LLMs evaluate (Gu et al., 2025). Many studies (Zheng et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2025) have discussed biases such as position, verbosity, and self-enhancement. Others (Koo et al., 2024) high-light compassion fade, bandwagon effect, and at-tentional bias. Additional work (Chen et al., 2024b; Stureborg et al., 2024) has noted fallacy-oversight, authority, and beauty bias. Ye et al. (2025) propose an automated pipeline to measure diverse biases, while Wei et al. (2025) analyze biases across differ-ent prompt templates. However, this topic remains underexplored in the multimodal domain. While modality bias, where multimodal LLMs over-rely on language (Zheng et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025), is well known, its impact on judging behavior remains un-clear. The most related work, Li et al. (2025c), shows that over-relying on text-only patterns dur-ing training hurts the generalization of multimodal reward models. Our work goes further by linking this issue to over-prioritizing informativeness. 

2.3 Debiasing in LLM/VLM-as-a-Judge 

To reduce bias in LLM-as-a-Judge, studies have asked judges to follow predefined rules (Zeng et al., 2024), provided reference answers to avoid con-text confusion (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2025a), swapped response positions to reduce po-sitional bias (Wang et al., 2024a), or equalized an-swer length to reduce length bias (Hu et al., 2025). However, these methods are not designed to handle informativeness bias and are therefore ineffective for this issue. On the multimodal side, Li et al. (2025c) re-duces bias from textual spurious correlations by shifting the training distribution toward better mul-timodal understanding. Our work instead identifies informativeness bias as a cause and addresses it by enforcing attention to image consistency without additional training.

3.3 Judging Informativeness 

General-purpose alignment aims to produce re-sponses that balance correctness and informative-ness (Zheng et al., 2023). This is reflected in com-mon judging prompts, which often include rubrics such as: "Your evaluation should consider factors such as accuracy, helpfulness, relevance, depth, and level of detail" (Zheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a), encouraging responses to go beyond a min-imal correct answer and provide additional context (Lin et al., 2022). Here, accuracy corresponds to correctness, which requires using the image to de-tect errors or inconsistencies, while helpfulness, relevance, depth, and level of detail correspond to informativeness, which can be judged from the answers alone. We focus on how informativeness affects VLM-as-a-Judge behavior, so we separate it from the other judging criteria. Based on this, we define the rubrics for informa-tiveness as helpfulness, relevance, depth, and level of detail , with accuracy removed from the original set. We then ask GPT-4o to decide which answer, 

yc or yr, is more informative using these rubrics. The full prompt is in Appendix A. Only the textual query Q and the two candidate answers (yc, y r) are given, while the image I is withheld, since informa-tiveness does not depend on it. GPT-4o is explicitly instructed to judge only on informativeness, with-out considering correctness or consistency with the image. The output is yi, the more informative an-swer, where yi is either yc or yr. See Appendix C.1 for an evaluation of the reliability of GPT-4o in measuring informativeness

3.5 Measuring Length Bias 

Prior studies show that LLM-as-a-Judge often fa-vors longer responses, known as length bias (Zheng et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2025). Since informativeness is often tied to length, with more informative an-swers usually being longer, we also measure Length Bias (LB) for comparison to investigate how the two biases relate. Following the definition of informativeness bias, we split the dataset D into two subsets, one where the longer answer is preferred and one where the shorter answer is preferred. We then calculate LB as the difference in a judge’s accuracy between them: LB = Acc yc is longer − Acc yc is shorter . 4.2 Judges Are Deceived by Informativeness Judges over-prioritize informativeness. As shown in Figure 3, we find significant informative-ness bias (IB): over 30% for Gemini-2.5-Flash and over 50% for other models. We also report IB vari-ance by downsampling DIDS and DCDS at different ratios (see Appendix C.3), showing reasonably low variance. The high IB is also clear in Figure 9, where Acc IDS is always much higher than Acc CDS .These indicate that VLM judges perform far worse when informativeness is misleading, revealing their strong tendency to overvalue informativeness. 

Judge performance heavily depends on how of-ten informativeness is misleading. We downsam-ple to control the ratio of the correctness-driven sub-set DCDS and examine how performance changes with it. 4.3 Informativeness Bias Pulls Judge’s Attention Away from the Image 

From Section 4.1 and 4.2, we hypothesize that in-formativeness bias (IB) distracts the judge’s atten-tion from the image, leading to a low image re-liance score (IRS). We now verify this connection. 

Image reliance is higher on correctness-driven cases DCDS . As shown in Figure 5, IRS is con-sistently higher on correctness-driven cases DCDS 

than on informativeness-driven cases DIDS across all models and benchmarks, indicating that judges rely more on images when correctness drives the decision. This supports our hypothesis: when in-formativeness is misleading, correctness becomes crucial, and as correctness cannot be verified with-out the image, visual input is far more valuable. 

Informativeness bias worsens when the judge lacks image input. We test how removing the im-age from the judge’s input affects IB. As shown in Figure 6, judges without images consistently show stronger IB than those with images, with gaps of up to 18.6%. This also supports our claim: without the image, the judge cannot verify correctness and is forced to rely only on informativeness. 

4.4 Informativeness Bias Is Distinct from Length Bias 

Figure 3 compares informativeness bias (IB) with length bias (LB). LB is consistently much weaker, trailing IB by about 30% or more across all models and benchmarks. Figure 8 further breaks this down and shows IB is higher than LB in nearly all do-mains. This suggests that IB is not merely a proxy for LB; rather, it might be a more substantial issue. To investigate further, we remove the effect of LB by creating a version of the MLLM-as-a-Judge benchmark where the length difference between candidate answers is below a small threshold (see Appendix C.5). Table 2 shows IB after this length equalization. While IB decreases, it remains sub-stantial (26%-45.5%). This suggests that, although IB and LB are correlated, IB cannot be resolved by addressing LB alone, as a substantial portion of IB remains after controlling for length. The correla-tion is expected, since more informative answers are often longer, making a preference for informa-tiveness appear as a preference for length. However, mitigating IB requires directly addressing informa-tiveness bias rather than focusing only on length effects (Zheng et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2025). 

5 Method 

Based on Section 4, VLM-as-a-Judge can answer questions correctly but gets distracted by informa-tiveness and fails to focus on the image. Therefore, we explore whether using its own answer as a ref-erence can refocus attention on image consistency. Unlike prior work that improves LLM-as-a-Judge by providing ground-truth references (Zheng et al., 2023; Hagos et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2025), we instead use a self-generated reference. 

5.1 Reducing Informativeness Bias 

Figure 12 illustrates the Standard Ref pipeline. The model first answers the question directly, en-coding key image details relevant to the question into a textual reference. It then judges by com-paring the two candidate answers to this reference. This naturally shifts the focus from informative-ness alone to consistency with the image, since the reference already includes image details. Figure 12 (A) shows a successful case: with its own answer as reference, the judge correctly de-tects that Answer B gives a park name not visible in the image and selects Answer A instead. Table 4 confirms that this greatly reduces informativeness bias (IB) and improves Acc CDS , boosting perfor-mance when correctness should drive the decision. However, this approach can also cause judges to over-prioritize correctness and unfairly reject valid details in candidates simply because they are missing from the reference. In Figure 12 (B), An-swer A is rejected just for mentioning the correct position of a sign omitted in the reference. Table 4 shows a clear drop in Acc IDS , meaning the judge performs worse when informativeness should guide the decision. This matches prior findings (Wu et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2023) that greater emphasis on correctness often leads to over-caution about ex-tra information. As a result, while this method improves Acc CDS and lowers IB, it still reduces overall accuracy compared to the base pipeline. 

5.2 Our Method: BIRCH 

We propose BIRCH, which balances informative-ness and correctness using an "anchor" that is both truthful and as informative as the candidates. As shown in Figure 7, the judge first modifies each can-didate answer by correcting inconsistencies with the image or removing unsupported claims with ex-planations. The corrected answers are then merged into a single anchor. This ensures the generated anchor encodes the truthful version of all relevant image details included in the candidates. As a re-sult, when candidates are compared to the anchor, every detail can be verified against it, preserving informativeness while enforcing correctness. 

6 Experiments 

6.1 Evaluation Settings 

We evaluate our approach on MLLM-as-a-Judge (Chen et al., 2024a) and VL-RewardBench (Li et al., 2025b) benchmarks. 6.3 Why BIRCH Works Well? Quantitative Results. We analyze changes across multiple metrics with and without BIRCH in Table 2 and 8. BIRCH shows a much lower IB than both 

Base and Standard Ref across all models. For GPT-4o, BIRCH boosts Acc CDS by 19% over Base ,much higher than the gain from Standard Ref ,and also raises Acc IDS by 4.8% at the same time, resulting in a 9.3% overall accuracy gain. In con-trast, Standard Ref causes a 4.6% drop in Acc IDS ,which finally lowers overall accuracy. These re-sults show that BIRCH maintains a good balance without over-prioritizing or over-penalizing infor-mativeness. Moreover, BIRCH also clearly reduces length bias (LB), consistent with our finding that IB and LB are not independent and that mitigating IB also alleviates LB. 

Case study. We provide qualitative examples in Figure 7, 12, 13 and Appendix D to illustrate how 

BIRCH balances informativeness and correctness, and why Standard Ref and Image Caption fail. 

Base is easily misled by a more informative but in-correct answer, even when it clearly conflicts with the judge’s own response. BIRCH creates an anchor by correcting or removing inconsistencies while preserving valid details. This allows the judge to verify every detail in the candidates and make cor-rect judgments. In contrast, Standard Ref and 

Image Caption generate references without using the candidate answers and often miss details men-tioned in them. As a result, Standard Ref makes the judge overly cautious and reject correct details that are not in the reference, while Image Caption 

accepts unsupported details when they cannot be verified against the caption. BIRCH reduces both incorrect rejection and acceptance and achieves a better balance. 

6.4 Anchor Quality 

The effectiveness of BIRCH depends on the quality of the generated anchors. These anchors should contain fewer factual errors than the original candi-date answers to help the judge verify image details. To evaluate this, we use GPT-5 to score the correct-ness of the anchors and the two original candidate answers for Gemini-2.5-Flash and LLaVA-1.5-13B. As shown in Table 5, the anchors achieve higher av-erage scores than both original answers, indicating that VLMs can correct many image inconsistencies and produce more accurate anchors. 

6.5 Inference Effencify 

Although anchor generation in BIRCH involves two steps, answer correction and merging, they are im-plemented in a single pass, as shown in Table 20. Therefore, BIRCH requires only one additional pass compared to the plain judge pipeline Base .We further illustrate this by reporting the aver-age inference time per data point in seconds for LLaVA-One-Vision-1.5-8B on a single A100 GPU for BIRCH and Base in Table 6. The inference time of BIRCH is about twice that of Base , which is ex-pected since it performs one extra pass. This over-head is comparable to or lower than many LLM-as-a-judge methods (Tang et al., 2024; Khan et al., 2024) that require multiple extra calls. 

7 Conclusion 

We reveal and address a fundamental flaw in cur-rent VLM-as-a-Judge systems, namely informative-ness bias , the tendency to blindly favor more infor-mative answers while giving little attention to the image. To improve reliability, we propose BIRCH, which compares candidate answers against a self-generated anchor that corrects or verifies all image details mentioned. This shifts the judge’s focus back to image content, ensuring every detail is prop-erly verified and thus balancing informativeness with correctness. BIRCH substantially reduces in-formativeness bias and delivers consistent gains across multiple VLMs and benchmarks. 8 Limitations 

Our work analyzes the issues that undermine the re-liability of VLM-as-a-Judge and proposes BIRCH to reduce informativeness bias and refocus on im-age content for more truthful judgments. BIRCH is simple to implement and requires no extra training. However, informativeness bias still remains after applying BIRCH (Table 3), which shows that it does not fully resolve the problem. Future work could address the following issues. BIRCH builds an anchor by correcting candidate answers to en-code key image details and guide the judge toward correctness. However, the model can still be in-fluenced by informativeness during this process and fail to remove or fix incorrect claims, which reduces the quality of the anchor. Besides, even when BIRCH generates a reliable and accurate an-chor, the judge may still favor a more informative but incorrect answer (Appendix D.3). In addition, BIRCH is designed to boost perfor-mance by reducing informativeness bias. However, for models like Phi-4-Multimodal, where the bias is already small (Section C.6), the gains are lim-ited (Table 3). Future work should explore ways to further strengthen judging ability in such models. 

9 Ethical Considerations 

In this paper, we introduce BIRCH, a new judg-ing framework designed to make VLM-as-a-Judge more reliable and better aligned with human prefer-ences. However, human annotators themselves are subject to bias, which in turn makes aligning VLM-as-a-Judge with their preferences also biased. We leave mitigating such biases in both benchmarks and VLM-as-a-Judge to future work.

B Benchmark Details 

B.1 Introduction MLLM-as-a-Judge (Chen et al., 2024a) is the first benchmark to evaluate multimodal LLMs as judges. It contains 4,414 image-instruction pairs from 14 datasets covering tasks such as image cap-tioning, math reasoning, text reading, and info-graphic understanding. Candidate answers are gen-erated using six MLLMs: GPT-4V (OpenAI et al., 2024a), Gemini-Pro-Vision (Team et al., 2025), LLaVA-1.5-13b, LLaVA-1.6-34b (Liu et al., 2024), CogVLM (Wang et al., 2024c), and Qwen-VL-Max (Wang et al., 2024b). The benchmark provides three settings: (1) scoring evaluation, where each answer is rated from 1 to 5, (2) pairwise compari-son, where two answers are compared to select the better one, and (3) batch ranking, where answers are ordered by quality. In our work, we use only the pairwise comparison setting, which includes 7,756 samples. 

VL-RewardBench (Li et al., 2025b) focuses on the pairwise comparison setting, with 1,247 sam-ples from 7 datasets. It includes hallucination-oriented queries in addition to general instruction-following and reasoning tasks, and provides error-type labels. To ensure the samples are challeng-ing enough to expose model weaknesses, it only keeps cases where multiple smaller models (such as LLaVA-1.5/1.6-7B (Liu et al., 2024) and Qwen2-VL-7B (Wang et al., 2024b)) make errors. B.2 Preprocessing 

We found that human annotators made many errors when labeling the better candidate answer in the benchmarks we used. Figures 14, 15, 16 and 17 show examples of such errors. These mistakes undermine the reliability of evaluating VLM-as-a-Judge. To address this, we manually reviewed the data samples and corrected the identified errors before evaluation.
