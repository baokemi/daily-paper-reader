# Buying Data of Unknown Quality: Fisher Information Procurement Auctions 

Yuchen Hu, Martin J. Wainwright, Stephen Bates MIT 

April 13, 2026 

Abstract 

We study statistical parameter estimation in the setting of data markets. A buyer seeks to estimate a parameter based on samples that can be purchased from competing providers that differ in their data quality and provision costs. When quality is known ex ante, we define a cost-per-information score that summarizes each provider’s provision cost per unit of information about the buyer’s estimation objective. We describe second-score procurement mechanism that ranks providers by this score, and endogenously chooses both a provider and a sample size while making truthful cost reports optimal. We then turn to the more realistic setting where data quality is private, and can only be indirectly observed via the delivered data. In this setting, we propose a simple mechanism that augments the second-score rule with a lenient ex post statistical test of the reported quality. We prove that under mild conditions, there exists an equilibrium in which sellers report costs truthfully and report quality up to deviations that vanish as the procured sample size grows. Our analysis highlights how the choice of verification test and the buyer’s accuracy-cost tradeoff jointly shape participation and misreporting incentives in data markets. 

# 1 Introduction 

Many organizations now purchase data at scale, either by contracting with external providers or by outsourcing parts of their data collection to specialized teams (Liang et al., 2018). Data providers differ widely in both the quality and cost of the data they can supply: some data sets are cheap to obtain but noisy or weakly informative, while others are expensive because they require careful measurement, recruiting hard-to-reach populations, or running complex experiments (Haug et al., 2011). Yet, before any contract is signed, it is difficult to know how informative a provider’s data will be, and it is even harder to verify the provider’s internal cost of collection. This information asymmetry raises a central question for data procurement: how can a buyer design a mechanism that elicits sellers’ private information, while deciding from whom to buy, how much data to acquire, and how much to pay? For eliciting costs, second-price auctions are by now classical tools: when each seller has a single private cost parameter, these mechanisms make truthful cost reporting a weakly dominant strategy and select the cheapest provider (Vickrey, 1961; Myerson, 1981). However, applying this logic to data procurement is more subtle. When purchasing data, the buyer must decide not only which seller to buy from, but also how much data to purchase, and the optimal quantity depends not only on cost but also on data quality. In certain settings, it can be better to buy a smaller amount of high-quality data at a higher per-unit price than a large amount of low-quality, inexpensive data. At the same time, each seller’s data quality is hidden, which renders the auction problem multi-dimensional. It is thus in general difficult to enforce truthful reporting via a single scalar 1

1.1 Our contributions 

In this paper, we study data procurement in a stylized model. Suppose that a single buyer wishes to estimate a scalar parameter (e.g., the mean of a distribution), and can do so by purchasing observations from a collection of M potential providers. Each provider i ∈ [M ] := {1, . . . , M }

has a private cost ci associated with producing data, as well as a private measure Vi of data informativeness or quality, and the buyer’s objective is to balance statistical accuracy against total procurement cost. We first show that in the ideal setting of known data informativeness, the buyer’s problem can be summarized by a “price per information” score: it ranks providers by how expensive it is to obtain a unit of information from them. Using this score as a per-unit price for information, we design a benchmark second-score mechanism that chooses the provider with the lowest per-unit price and then buys from the winner the amount of information that would be optimal at the per-unit price set by the runner-up. Because both the information quantity and the payment are tied to this runner-up price rather than to the winner’s own report, truthful cost revelation is weakly dominant even though the amount of data purchased is endogenously determined. In practice, of course, data informativeness is not known ex ante. Instead, it can often only be inferred from the data that end up being procured. Our second contribution is to show how the buyer can exploit this noisy ex post information while retaining the simplicity of the second-score structure. When the buyer must rely on sellers’ reports about quality as well as cost, we augment the benchmark mechanism with a simple statistical test applied to the realized data: if the delivered quality falls too far below the report, the contract is voided but the seller still bears their collection cost. Under mild conditions, we show that there exists a Bayesian Nash equilibrium in which all participating sellers report qualities in a neighborhood of the truth while continuing to report costs truthfully. Furthermore, when the buyer procures a sufficiently large sample, this equilibrium becomes almost truthful and individually rational for all but the sellers with extremely high cost per information, so the mechanism effectively behaves like a truthful data-procurement procedure. Our results also shed light on how the choice of statistical test and the accuracy-cost tradeoff shape incentives, and provide practical guidance on how much data a buyer should purchase in order to sustain near-truthful behavior when quality can only be verified noisily. 

1.2 Related work 

Our work builds on the classic literature in auction theory and mechanism design (Laffont and Martimort, 2002). Truthful mechanism design originates from the Vickrey–Clarke–Groves (VCG) framework (Vickrey, 1961; Clarke, 1971; Groves, 1973), which characterizes efficient and incentive-compatible mechanisms under quasi-linear preferences. Myerson (1981) further provides a general theory of optimal auctions in single-parameter environments that maximize the seller’s expected revenue under incentive and participation constraints. These frameworks have since inspired a broad class of contracting mechanisms that aim to elicit truthful cost reports while maximizing allocation efficiency or minimizing expenditure. The procurement setting we study is a natural dual of the auction problem, where a buyer seeks to minimize procurement costs while ensuring sufficient quality of the procured goods or 2services (Laffont and Tirole, 1993). In these settings, the buyer faces the reverse problem of Myerson’s auction: rather than allocating goods to bidders with the highest virtual values, the principal selects suppliers with the lowest virtual costs. In procurement auctions, the principal often needs to balance price and quality considerations (Klein and Leffler, 1981; Manelli and Vincent, 1995; Asker and Cantillon, 2010; Yao and Tanaka, 2020). A key challenge arises when the buyer does not know the supplier’s quality ex ante. Prior work has addressed this by modeling procurement with costly pre-contract qualification of suppliers (Wan and Beil, 2009; Chen et al., 2018); by studying mechanisms with exact ex post verification (Ben-Porath et al., 2014; Mylovanov and Zapechelnyuk, 2017); and by leveraging relational and reputational enforcement, such as contract termination or discriminatory scoring mechanisms in repeated relationships (Board, 2011; Auriol and Søreide, 2017; Albano et al., 2017). Our study contributes to this line of research by proposing a procurement mechanism with statistical quality verification, where the buyer cross-checks the reported data quality using noisy empirical information estimates and voids the contract when the verification fails. Perhaps most closely related to our work is the growing literature on data procurement for improving the performance of statistical estimation and machine learning models. Like this work, many of these studies build upon Myerson’s seminal framework and adapt it to address challenges unique to data acquisition. One prevalent design for data procurement with multiple agents is to use peer prediction (see, e.g., Miller et al. 2005; Richardson et al. 2019; Chen et al. 2020; Clinton et al. 2025 and references therein), which leverages mutual information between reports to make truth-telling an equilibrium. Beyond static designs, recent work has studied data procurement mechnisms in online settings, where the principal sequentially acquires data while balancing exploration, budget constraints, and incentive compatibility (Abernethy et al., 2015; Cao et al., 2017; An et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2018; Chen and Zheng, 2019; Li et al., 2021). Our work is also closely related to research on data collection delegation (Cai et al., 2015; Gast et al., 2020; Ananthakrishnan et al., 2024; Saig et al., 2023), in which data providers act as “workers” who can improve the quality of their data through costly effort, and mechanisms are designed to incentivize such effort so that high-quality data are produced at low cost for statistical estimation. In contrast, we consider a setting where the quality of each agent’s data is fixed and exogenous, and the principal’s goal is to select agents with inherently higher data quality rather than incentivizing them to improve it. The mechanism we study relies on a verification test of the amount of statistical information contained in the reported data, compared to the information implied by the agent’s report. In this spirit, our work is related to information elicitation. For prediction and estimation, proper scoring rules have been widely used to elicit truthful reports of probabilistic forecasts and sufficient statistics (Gneiting and Raftery, 2007; Lambert et al., 2008; Frankel and Kamenica, 2019). For decision making, they have been extended to design mechanisms that align incentives with optimal actions or posterior beliefs in decision-theoretic settings (Osband, 1989; Chen and Kash, 2011; Shi et al., 2024, 2025), and, in particular, to develop decision scoring rules that incentivize agents to truthfully report costly information (Oesterheld and Conitzer, 2020; Neyman et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Papireddygari and Waggoner, 2022). Compared with these works, we study a more challenging setting in which agents may benefit from misreporting the information required for the principal’s decision by an unknown and unobservable amount, which we mitigate through an auction-based design. Finally, at a high level, our work contributes to the growing literature on mechanism design for data sharing and data markets (Liang et al., 2018; Castro Fernandez, 2023). Much of this literature 3focuses on developing fair and truth-incentivizing data valuation strategies implemented through centralized data-access platforms or third-party intermediaries (Jia et al., 2019; Ghorbani and Zou, 2019; Yoon et al., 2020; Wang and Jia, 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Just et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), as well as on privacy-preserving mechanisms for data procurement, exchange, and aggregation (Ghosh and Roth, 2011; Fleischer and Lyu, 2012; Li et al., 2014; Anjarlekar et al., 2023; Cummings et al., 2023; Fallah et al., 2024). In contrast, we study a decentralized data procurement problem, where data remain privately held by self-interested agents, and the principal must design incentives to elicit and verify data quality without relying on a trusted intermediary.

The next section formalizes both the Bayesian framework and an asymptotic sequence along which the random sample size grows, and derives our main results on equilibrium behavior and participation under noisy verification. 10 3.3 Bayesian Equilibrium Analysis with Noisy Quality Verification 

In this section, we place Mechanism 2 in a standard Bayesian game-theoretic environment (e.g., Harsanyi (1967)) and formalize the noisy verification setting described in Section 3.2. Given some mild reg-ularity conditions, we show that there exists a Bayesian Nash equilibrium in which, conditional on participating, each agent is guaranteed to (a) report its cost truthfully, and (b) give a data quality report that belongs to a shrinking interval around the truth. Furthermore, for agents whose equilibrium winning score maintains a non-trivial gap over the runner-up’s score, we show that such (almost) truthful reports also weakly dominate opting out. Thus, in large-sample regimes, agents are (almost) truthful, and we recover the performance of the second-price-per-information mechanism with known quality, up to a small error. To formalize the Bayesian game, recall that each agent i is associated with a type ti = ( ci, Vi) ∈ T ,where ci is the per-sample cost and Vi is the inverse Fisher information. In the Bayesian setting, types are drawn independently from a common prior distribution F on T , and this prior is common knowledge. Given their type ti, seller i chooses an action ai ∈ A out := A ∪ { out }, where actions in A are participation reports ai = ( pi, eVi) and the special action “out” corresponds to opting out. A (possibly mixed) reporting strategy for seller i is a measurable mapping ri : T → ∆( Aout ) that assigns to each type ti a distribution over actions. Let r = ( r1, . . . , r M ) denote a strategy profile across all M agents, and write Ui(ai, a −i; ti) for seller i’s utility when they choose action ai, their competitors chooses action a−i, and their true type is ti.Throughout this section, we impose the following regularity assumptions on types and bids. These are standard in private-value Bayesian auction models (Myerson, 1981), and are important for our equilibrium existence result give in Section 3.3.1. 

Assumption 1. There exist constants 0 < c < c < ∞ and 0 < V < V < ∞ such that, for each agent i ∈ [M ], their type ti = ( ci, Vi) takes values in T := [ c, c ] × [V, V], and the prior distribution over types has full support on the M -fold Cartesian product space T M . Furthermore, agent i’s reporting action ai = ( pi, eVi) is restricted to A = [ c, c ] × [V, V]. 

Assumption 2. Given the type distribution and any measurable strategy profile, the induced distribution of scores has no atoms; in particular, ties occur with probability zero.

3.3.2 Equilibrium Participation and Approximate Incentive Compatibility 

Above, we show that any agent who opts into the mechanism reports their cost truthfully and their quality within a shrinking interval around the truth in an equilibrium. This result describes how reports behave conditional on participation, but it does not yet tell us which agents choose to participate rather than opting out. In this section, we show that, under a mild non-degeneracy condition, participation is indeed individually rational for agents whose equilibrium winning score maintains a non-trivial gap over the runner-up’s score. In Mechanism 2, the only reason why an agent might incur a negative utility (and strongly prefer opting out) is that their expected winning utility is negative. This can happen when the expected loss from failing the verification test cannot be offset by the surplus they obtain when they win and the test passes. Thus, the participation decision is governed by two key quantities: the probability of failing the test, and the expected gap between an agent’s own score and the runner-up’s score once they win. In particular, if an agent’s surplus conditional on winning is always zero, for instance because their score is always tied with the second-best score, then the expected utility from participating is always non-positive. # 5 Discussion 

We take a step toward a theory of data procurement as information acquisition under limited prior knowledge of providers’ costs and data quality. Our mechanism combines a selection rule from second-score procurement auctions with an enforcement device in the spirit of scoring-rule-based forecast evaluation. By leveraging competition and a noisy ex post signal about the in-formation delivered, it implements an almost truthful and individually rational data-procurement procedure—something that is hard to obtain with purely auction-based or purely scoring-rule-based mechanisms. Our analysis has focused on a scalar information setting, where each agent’s data quality is captured by a one-dimensional information metric, such as how noisy their data are. In this case, for any fixed pricing scheme, the purchasing rule that minimizes the principal’s loss is a winner-take-all one, where it is optimal to buy all samples from a single agent even though the principal could, in principle, buy from multiple agents. This property inherently enables us to design a simple second-score mechanism that always ensures an optimal procurement quantity. In many applications, however, the optimal design would involve purchasing data from multiple agents, for example, when the parameter of interest is vector-valued and different agents are informative about different components or regions of the covariate space, or when the principal wants to guard against source-specific biases by combining data from multiple agents. Extending our framework to such 18 settings, where the optimal allocation may buy positive quantities from multiple agents rather than always from a single source, would require mechanisms that jointly determine prices and quantities across all data providers, and we leave this as an interesting direction for future work.
