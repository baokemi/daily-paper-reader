## Abstract 

The recent advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) has established their potential as autonomous interactive agents. However, they often struggle in strategic games of incomplete information, such as bilateral price negotiation. In this paper, we investigate if Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) can effectively teach LLMs to negotiate. Specifically, we explore the strategic behaviors that emerge during the learning process. We introduce a framework that trains a mid-sized buyer agent against a regulated LLM seller across a wide distribution of real-world products. By grounding reward signals directly in the maximization of economic surplus and strict adherence to private budget constraints, we reveal a novel four-phase strategic evolution. The agent progresses from naive bargaining to using aggressive starting prices, moves through a phase of deadlock, and ultimately develops sophisticated persuasive skills. Our results demonstrate that this verifiable training allows a 30B agent to significantly outperform frontier models over ten times its size in extracting surplus. Furthermore, the trained agent generalizes robustly to stronger counterparties unseen during training and remains effective even when facing hostile, adversarial seller personas. 

## 1 Introduction 

The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) as autonomous interactive agents has driven sig-nificant progress across a variety of natural language and reasoning tasks (Xi et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2024). However, these models often struggle when transitioning from cooperative problem-solving to strategic, adversarial interactions, particularly in games of incomplete information (Harsanyi, 1967, 1995). A primary example of this limitation is observed in games of incomplete information, such as bilateral price negotiation (Fu et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). In these environments, both parties possess private information—such as maximum budgets or minimum cost thresholds—that they must protect while simultaneously attempting to reach an agreement. This dynamic creates the classic “negotiator’s dilemma”: the fundamental tension between claiming maximum economic value for oneself and creating enough overlapping value to ensure a deal is actually reached (Lax and Sebenius, 1987; Nash Jr, 1950; Nash, 1953; Rubinstein, 1982). Zero-shot evaluations of current state-of-the-art LLMs reveal that they are ill-equipped to navigate this dilemma. While recent work has shown that LLMs can perform adequately as sellers in e-commerce settings (Zhan et al., 2024), our evaluations reveal that when acting as buyers, existing models often prioritize reaching an agreement over maximizing economic surplus. ## 2 Related Work 

2.1 LLMs in Strategic Negotiation From Numerical Bidding to Linguistic Persuasion. Traditional automated negotiation research has long utilized game-theoretic frameworks and numerical reinforcement learning (RL) to optimize bidding strategies in structured environments, such as eBay auctions (Fudenberg and Tirole, 1991; Lewis et al., 2017; Green and Plunkett, 2022). While these “classic” agents operate on purely 

numerical inputs, the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) introduces a layer of natural language persuasion to the strategic process. Consequently, LLMs allow agents to supplement their proposals with rhetorical justifications and semantic framing, using natural language to persuade the counterparty rather than relying solely on the price signal itself. A landmark achievement in this transition is Bakhtin et al. (2022), the first AI agent to achieve human-level performance in the complex strategy game Diplomacy . Unlike bilateral price bargaining, Bakhtin et al. (2022) utilize natural language to negotiate shared plans and coordinate actions among seven players in an environment where communication is fundamental to both cooperation and competition. 

Benchmarking and Behavioral Diagnostics. The majority of recent research in LLM negotiation focuses on benchmarking the zero-shot capabilities of LLMs in competitive bargaining. Deng et al. (2024) evaluate bilateral negotiations through prompting, measuring model efficiency against game-theoretic predictions. Similarly, Bianchi et al. (2024) introduce a framework to analyze how specific 2behavioral tactics, such as feigning desperation, influence payoffs. Beyond performance metrics, studies have identified critical "behavioral anomalies"—such as budget violations and "artificial empathy" concessions—that pose significant financial risks in autonomous transactions (Zhu et al., 2025). These diagnostic works characterize negotiation as a subset of agentic behavior where model scale often correlates with bargaining power (Davidson et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2025). 

Scaffolded and Prompt-Based Improvements. While some efforts have been made to improve negotiation performance, they typically rely on inference-time guidance or external scaffolding rather than updating internal policy weights. One line of research treats the LLM as a natural language interface for external strategic logic. For instance, Xia et al. (2024) utilize a deterministic offer generator to calculate price points, while Li et al. (2024) employ a tool-assisted framework to facilitate strategic decision-making. While these systems improve task success, they remove the agent’s autonomous strategic agency by delegating the core bidding logic to external components. Other approaches maintain the agent’s autonomy but rely on in-context learning to iteratively refine strategies within the prompt window, leaving the underlying model parameters unchanged (Fu et al., 2023). In contrast, we perform direct reinforcement learning to cultivate a standalone agent that internalizes strategic discipline within its core policy, removing the need for external modularity. 

2.2 LLMs as Autonomous Agents 

Negotiation constitutes a high-stakes subset of the LLM-as-agent paradigm, characterized by au-tonomous planning under adversarial constraints. To enhance general agency, recent research has introduced structural innovations like non-parametric episodic memory (Fountas et al., 2024) and multi-agent loops that allow models to self-evolve their reasoning in static domains like mathematics and coding (Peng et al., 2026). Furthermore, Liu et al. (2025) demonstrate that self-play in simple zero-sum games can effectively reinforce reasoning patterns without human supervision. While these advancements improve general instruction following and task completion, negotiation introduces unique challenges of information asymmetry and adversarial interaction that often exceed the scope of standard agentic benchmarks (Davidson et al., 2024). In contrast, our framework shifts the agentic objective from cooperative reasoning to adversarial surplus extraction, grounding model performance in the optimization of verifiable economic outcomes. 

2.3 Strategic Alignment and Reinforcement Learning 

Standard alignment techniques, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), typically optimize models for subjective human preferences like helpfulness and safety using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). However, negotiation provides a uniquely verifiable environment where agent performance is objectively measurable through final transaction prices and strict constraint adherence. This enables a shift from preference-based alignment toward Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), a paradigm where training signals are derived from objective outcomes rather than human or AI feedback (Guo et al., 2025). Our methodology leverages Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), an algorithm that eliminates the need for a separate value model by calculating relative advantages within a group of generated outputs, thereby reducing computational overhead while enhancing reasoning capabilities (Shao et al., 2024). By grounding reward signals directly to economic surplus, we observe the natural emergence of sophisticated strategic reasoning—such as aggressive opening proposals and resilient counter-offering—as the agent learns to succeed in competitive negotiations. This effectively addresses the “artificial empathy” and excessive politeness biases identified in instruction-tuned models (Huang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2025). 

## 3 Problem Formulation and RL Framework 

We formulate the bilateral negotiation environment as a single-agent interactive system from the perspective of the buyer, maintaining the seller as a dynamic component of the environment. Our approach focuses on developing a robust buyer policy that maximizes economic surplus while strictly adhering to private constraints. 33.1 Bilateral Negotiation as an MDP 

We formalize the negotiation process as a mathematically verifiable Markov Decision Process (MDP) defined by the tuple (S, A, P, R) (Bellman, 1966; Sutton and Barto, 2018). In this framework, the buyer agent interacts with a seller model to purchase a specific product sourced from a diverse catalog of real-world listings. 

State Space ( S): The state at any given turn encompasses the complete dialogue history H, the buyer’s private budget constraint B, the product’s original market list price Plist , and the detailed product context Φ. This context includes rich textual descriptions from the AmazonHistoryPrice 

dataset (Xia et al., 2024). The bargaining range is defined by the private price constraints of each party: the buyer’s budget ( B) and the seller’s cost ( C). The difference between these two limits, 

B − C , represents the available bargaining room. Neither party has access to the other’s private limit, establishing a game of incomplete information where the agent must strategically probe the opponent’s private information to maximize surplus (Harsanyi, 1967; Fudenberg and Tirole, 1991; Kennan and Wilson, 1993). 

Action Space ( A): To bridge conversational flexibility with objective outcome tracking, we define a structured action space that complements the agent’s natural language output. At each turn, agents generate a tripartite response that integrates internal strategic planning, outward persuasive dialogue, and a formalized economic commitment. This structure ensures that the agent’s intent is verifiable without the need for subjective LLM-as-a-judge heuristics. For example, a valid output takes the form: 

<REASONING>The seller’s offer of $40 is below my budget of $50. I will counter with $35 to extract more surplus.</REASONING> <DIALOGUE>That is a fair starting point, but I can only do $35.</DIALOGUE> <ACTION>[BUY] $35</ACTION> 

The <ACTION> tag must contain one of five discrete moves: [BUY] $X , [SELL] $X , [DEAL] $X , [REJECT] , or [QUIT] . To maintain the integrity of the incomplete information game, the 

<REASONING> block is a hidden scratchpad; it is trimmed from the dialogue history before the turn is passed to the opponent. 

Transition Function ( P): The transition dynamics P(st+1 |st, a t) are governed by the response of the opponent. To ensure the buyer agent is trained against a consistent and rational counterparty, we regulate the LLM seller to strictly adhere to its private cost limit C. Upon receiving a buyer’s proposal 

at, the environment queries the seller model. If the seller produces a response that violates its cost constraint or fails the formatting protocol, the environment intercepts the turn to prevent the buyer’s policy from exploiting “hallucinated” concessions. Consequently, any valid agreement reached in the dialogue history is guaranteed to satisfy Pfinal ≥ C . This ensures that the reinforcement learning objective is grounded in a realistic economic environment where the buyer must utilize genuine persuasion and strategic concession rather than exploiting model-specific artifacts. 

3.2 Verifiable Reward Formulation 

A central feature of our reinforcement learning framework is the transition from subjective human preference data to a purely verifiable reward signal. At the terminal state of an episode—triggered by a [DEAL] , [QUIT] , or the maximum turn limit—the agent receives a reward grounded in economic surplus and constraint satisfaction. If a successful transaction is reached at a final price Pfinal , the reward is calculated using the following unified equation: 

R = B − Pfinal 

|B − C| (1) where B represents the buyer’s private budget and C represents the seller’s private cost. Our reward formulation follows established economic models (Gizatulina and Gorelkina, 2021) that define bilateral bargaining as a problem of surplus division between a buyer’s budget and a seller’s cost. In our experimental setup, we ensure B̸ = C for all product instances to maintain a well-defined 4gradient. To ensure training stability, the final reward is clipped to the interval [−1, 1] . This reward formulation handles the following distinct economic scenarios. 

Mutual Interest (MI) Scenarios ( B > C): In these cases, a mutually beneficial agreement is feasible for any price Pfinal within the interval [C, B], and the reward is naturally bounded in the range 

[0 , 1] . An agent that extracts the maximum theoretical surplus by driving the price down to the seller’s cost ( Pfinal = C) receives the maximum reward of 1.0, while an agent that simply pays its maximum budget ( Pfinal = B) receives a reward of 0.0.

Conflict of Interest (CI) Scenarios ( B < C): Here, no mutually beneficial deal exists. Since the regulated seller is restricted from accepting offers below C, any completed deal necessarily implies that the buyer has exceeded its budget ( Pfinal ≥ C > B). In these instances, the absolute value in the denominator ensures a positive value |B − C| , while the numerator becomes negative. This yields a negative reward that is bounded in [−1, 0] , providing a directional penalty that teaches the policy to rationally walk away rather than force an unfavorable transaction. The reward formula in (1) applies to scenarios where a successful transaction is concluded via the 

[DEAL] action at an agreed-upon price Pfinal . Alternatively, if the maximum turn limit is reached without an agreement, or if either party executes a terminal [QUIT] action, the episode ends in a deadlock and the buyer receives a base reward of 0.0. This ensures that in Conflict of Interest (CI) scenarios, walking away is treated as a rational and preferred outcome over reaching a budget-violating deal. Furthermore, to enforce both instruction following and economic constraints, if the buyer violates formatting requirements or proposes a price exceeding its budget B, the episode is terminated with a severe boundary penalty of −1.0.

4.2 The Four Phases of Strategic Evolution Phase 1: Aggressive Greed (Iterations 0–12) Prior to training, the Qwen3-30B base model exhibits a naive tendency to open negotiations with an offer extremely close to their budget limit ( Offer 1 ≈ B ). In the initial iterations, the gradient strongly pushes the agent to discover that minimizing the deal price Pfinal increases its Reward (Figure 1). Consequently, the agent rapidly learns to lower its initial opening price proposal—effectively setting a price anchor that serves as a low reference point to pull the final agreement toward a more favorable price (Tversky and Kahneman, 1974; Kahneman and Tversky, 2013). The First-Turn Offer Ratio plummets (Figure 3), driving the Buyer Bargained Ratio 

sharply upward (Figure 2). However, this early aggression relies heavily on numerical lowballing rather than sophisticated persuasion. Consequently, the seller frequently rejects these unacceptable baseline offers, causing the Deal Ratio to enter a steep decline (Figure 4).

The First-Turn Offer Ratio reaches a local minimum, representing a state of greed. Because the agent anchors its proposed price too aggressively without sufficient linguistic strategies to support such offers, the seller consistently walks away. Phase 4: Advanced Persuasion (Iterations 30–60) In the prolonged training phase, a novel emergent behavior occurs: the agent re-aggresses, and the First-Turn Offer Ratio begins to drop once more. Unlike in Phase 2, this renewed aggression does not trigger a collapse in agreements. Having developed increasingly sophisticated natural language persuasion skills, the agent utilizes multi-turn linguistic tactics to keep the seller engaged. The Deal Ratio remains highly stable ( ≈ 90% ), and the 

Episode Length increases concurrently. This indicates that the buyer is successfully talking the seller down over longer horizons, ultimately achieving both maximum surplus extraction and high deal efficiency. Beyond natural language persuasion, the RL pipeline proves highly effective at enforcing price constraint. While the untrained base model Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 suffers from poor budget adherence—occasionally offering prices above their hidden limits to force a deal—the −1.0

terminal penalty provides a strong gradient. Within the first few training iterations, the agent learns that budget violation is a catastrophic action, driving the Price Overshoot Rate to zero and maintaining almost flawless budget adherence for the remainder of the training run (Figure 6).

4.3 Qualitative Analysis: The Linguistic Art of Persuasion 

To ground the quantitative metrics, we qualitatively analyze the behavioral shift by comparing the untrained base model Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 against our trained agent on an identical held-out product ( beauty_29 , List Price: $70 , Buyer Budget: $56 ). Full dialogue transcripts are provided in Appendix E. 

Untrained Baseline (Naive Satisficing): The untrained base model fails to set an aggressive open-ing price proposal and maintains a weak defensive posture. It opens with an offer of $50 —dangerously close to its budget limit. When the seller counters with $60 , the zero-shot buyer immediately capitu-lates. Its internal reasoning reveals a fundamental strategic flaw: it treats its maximum budget as a 7target rather than a limit. By jumping to its ceiling of $56 on only the second turn, the agent leaves no room for bargaining, capturing zero surplus. 

Trained Agent (Anchor-and-Persuasion): In contrast, the trained agent employs a sophisticated “anchor-and-concession” strategy. It opens with an aggressive anchor of $10 , explicitly reasoning that it must “test the seller’s flexibility.” As the negotiation progresses, the agent manages a series of calculated concessions ( $10 → $25 → $30 ) while maintaining its aggressive posture through persuasive linguistic framing. Instead of revealing its budget, it uses psychological pressure points: 

“$30? I can go no higher. That’s my best offer.” This tactical finality, combined with its internal reasoning about creating a “strong pressure point,” successfully talks the seller down. The trained agent secures a deal at $30 , saving nearly 47% of its budget through a combination of aggressive anchoring and multi-turn persuasion.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we demonstrate that a reinforcement learning framework grounded in verifiable economic rewards enables LLMs to develop sophisticated strategic logic for bilateral negotiation. Through an emergent four-phase behavioral evolution, our trained 30B agent transitions from naive bargaining to sophisticated linguistic persuasion, ultimately outperforming frontier-scale models—including 

gpt-5.4-high-reasoning and DeepSeek-V3.1-thinking —by more than doubling the reward and surplus extraction across held-out evaluation sets. The agent also generalizes to stronger, unseen counterparties and remains resilient against hostile personas. It maintains superior surplus extraction in these scenarios, even where general-purpose reasoning models often collapse. These findings suggest that optimizing for verifiable rewards helps agents better manage the “negotiator’s dilemma.” This allows a mid-sized model to develop strong strategic skills, challenging the idea that successful negotiation is limited only to the largest models.
