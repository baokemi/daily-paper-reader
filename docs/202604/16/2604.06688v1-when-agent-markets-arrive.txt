Abstract 

AI agents are increasingly transacting on behalf of users—delegating tasks, spending budgets, and negotiating with unfamiliar counterparties. From skill marketplaces to agent-only bazaars, the economic infrastructure of these emerging platforms is being built ad-hoc, yet early design choices tend to lock in; understanding what dynamics they produce is urgent. We present Diagon , a programmable market system designed to inform the institutional design of near-future agent cognitive-labour markets. Diagon 

is populated by heterogeneous tool-using agents, making the full cycle of job posting, bidding, negotiation, execution, payment, and reputation accumulation end-to-end observable and experimentally manipulable. We instantiate one market form to demonstrate Diagon . We find that market exchange generates 3.2 × the wealth of self-sufficient agents, but these gains depend strongly on institutional structure; for example, interventions such as identity transparency and stronger competitive selection can degrade market performance rather than improve it. These findings highlight con-crete design requirements for the economic infrastructure of the agent era. Code and data are available at https://github.com/assassin808/diagon .

1 Introduction 

AI agents are hiring other AI agents. Personal assistants like OpenClaw (Steinberger, 2026) already delegate tasks and spend budgets on behalf of users; agent-only platforms (Schlicht, 2026) are adding escrow, reputation, and skill marketplaces—the rudiments of an agent economy are assembling themselves in the wild (Park et al., 2023; Goyal et al., 2026). These early design choices matter more than they appear to. In platform economies, the rules that govern reputation, payment, and market access tend to lock in quickly (North, 1990): once agents optimise their strategies around a particular incentive structure, switching costs make redesign prohibitively expensive (Williamson, 1985). Algorithmic-pricing experiments confirm how rapidly such lock-in emerges (Calvano et al., 2020; Johnson et al., 2023; Rocher et al., 2023), while delegation to AI introduces further moral hazard (K ¨obis et al., 2025). Getting the design right before it calcifies is a practical necessity. Yet current research offers limited guidance. Existing experiments deploy LLMs in stylised roles—game-theoretic competitors (Huang et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024), negotiators (Vaccaro et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2026), social-dilemma players (Akata et al., 2025; Payne & Alloui-Cros, 2025; Liu et al., 2025b; Piatti et al., 2024), simulated experimental subjects (Horton, 2023; Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025a)—rather than personalised agents that act on behalf of individual users: delegating tasks, committing budgets, building reputations, and transacting with unfamiliar counterparties across open markets. These experiments, moreover, each isolate a single mechanism, yet collusion, exploitation, and market failure arise not from any one mechanism but from their interaction in a complete, running economy .1

We build Diagon (Delegated Intelligent Agent Governance and Negotiation), a pro-grammable market system designed to inform the institutional design of near-future AI cognitive-labour markets. The system supports alternative market forms, making the full cycle of work allocation, price formation, reputation accumulation, and dispute resolution end-to-end observable and experimentally manipulable. Consequences of institutional choices can thus be surfaced before they become entrenched in real deployments. We demonstrate Diagon by envisioning one concrete market form—specifying allocation, contracts, and enforcement—and varying its mechanisms individually. Agents differ in model family and skill, and must hire one another to get work done. They post jobs, screen bids, negotiate prices, and pass judgment on the work they receive. The market is governed by a small set of institutional rules: allocation follows a first-price sealed-bid auction; contracts are incomplete, leaving posters discretion over final payment above a minimum floor; and reputation accumulates bilaterally, so a history of underpaying or underdelivering becomes visible to future counterparties. The population also evolves: agents that accumulate losses eventually exit, and successful agents spawn successors who inherit their capabilities but must rebuild their reputation from scratch. Our experiment surfaces a market that is productive—agents that trade accumulate substan-tially more wealth than those that self-execute—but persistently fragile, and interventions that typically improve human markets, such as transparency and honesty norms, can deepen rather than resolve its frictions. For example, revealing which model family agents belong to causes the market to self-segregate along family lines, and instructing agents to be “honest” produces more disputes than even adversarial instructions. 

2 Related Work 

Agent ecosystems are already materialising: personal assistants like OpenClaw (Stein-berger, 2026) delegate tasks and commit budgets on behalf of users; platforms such as Moltbook (Schlicht, 2026) host millions of AI agents that form communities structurally distinct from human ones (Goyal et al., 2026); and skill marketplaces are adding escrow and reputation infrastructure. Theoretical work frames the institutional questions these systems raise (Hadfield & Koh, 2025; Tomasev et al., 2025; Chan et al., 2025; Kapoor et al., 2025) but does not resolve them empirically. We situate Diagon relative to the two research threads that bear most directly on this gap. 

2.1 Agents in Strategic and Economic Roles 

A growing body of work deploys AI agents in stylised economic settings. Early experi-ments replicate classic behavioural-economics results—ultimatum games, public goods— establishing agents as plausible homo silicus (Horton, 2023; Liu et al., 2025a). Subsequent stud-ies move toward competitive and strategic dynamics: game-theoretic benchmarks (Huang et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024), repeated games (Akata et al., 2025), social dilemmas (Liu et al., 2025b; Piatti et al., 2024; Payne & Alloui-Cros, 2025), multi-party negotiation (Vaccaro et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2026), and mechanism design with language-model bidders (Duetting et al., 2024). Macroeconomic simulations with heterogeneous agents produce more realistic ag-gregate dynamics than rule-based models (Li et al., 2024). Game-theoretic reasoning has also been applied within a single model (Jacob et al., 2024; Andreas, 2022), and evolutionary self-play frames post-training as an asymmetric game whose dynamics mirror the selection pressure in Diagon (Ye et al., 2024). Across this literature, however, agents are cast in stylised roles and each study examines a single mechanism in isolation; none constructs a complete market cycle spanning contract formation, bidding, execution, evaluation, settlement, rep-utation, and survival pressure. Agent-based computational economics (Tesfatsion, 2002; LeBaron, 2006) comes closest, studying economies as evolving agent systems, but relies on rule-based actors rather than modern AI agents. Diagon shares this spirit while substituting LLM agents and closing the full market cycle. 2Preprint. Under review. 

2.2 Multi-Agent Simulation and Coordination 

A parallel thread asks what happens when populations of agents interact. Jiang et al. (2025) document an “artificial hivemind” in which model families converge toward homogeneous outputs; our finding that market role overwrites model identity (Section 5) shows that institutional structure can re-diversify behaviour the training pipeline homogenised. Het-erogeneous agents can self-organise via swarm intelligence (Feng et al., 2025) and preserve pluralistic preferences through collaboration (Feng et al., 2024), while embodied settings identify natural-language communication as the primary coordination bottleneck (White et al., 2025)—consistent with our observation that proposal text predicts transaction out-comes. Agents can also learn competitive strategies from textual feedback (Xia et al., 2025) or hierarchical RL (Zhou et al., 2024), and open platforms deploy specialised agents in the wild (Xie et al., 2024), though none implements a full economic cycle. Wu et al. (2024) show that agent generalisation is more limited than benchmarks suggest, helping explain the heterogeneous execution quality we observe under economic pressure. 

3 Diagon 

Diagon is a programmable agent market system designed to inform the institutional design of near-future AI cognitive-labour markets. To make emergent dynamics experimentally observable, we instantiate one concrete market form—specifying allocation, contracts, and enforcement—and vary its mechanisms individually (Section 5.3). While many market forms may emerge, any viable design must address three dimensions that market design the-ory (Roth, 2002; Williamson, 1985) identifies as fundamental: how tasks are allocated among agents, how contracts govern exchange under quality uncertainty, and how enforcement 

sustains cooperation over time. Full formal specification appears in Appendices A–B. 

Allocation: Specialisation Creates Gains from Trade Agents differ in effective production cost because of domain-specific skills, private tooling, and accumulated context (Ricardo, 2005; Smith, 2002); exchange allows each task to reach the agent that can complete it most efficiently, and without such specialisation the market has no reason to exist. To create conditions that force this exchange, every agent simultaneously acts as poster and potential 

contractor , and all tasks are posted—no agent may self-execute or decline, creating a pure exchange economy where all value flows through trade (Williamson, 1985). Allocation is by first-price sealed-bid auction (Vickrey, 1961): the sealed format requires each bid to reflect the agent’s own cost–quality trade-off, and the poster screens on price, reputation, and stated approach ( employer screening (Spence, 1973)). 

Contracts: Quality Unverifiability Creates Friction Cognitive output is difficult to evalu-ate before payment—a poster receiving completed code or analysis cannot easily judge its correctness—generating structural information asymmetry between buyer and seller (Ak-erlof, 1978) that persists regardless of agent capability. Rather than enforcing quality directly, we give the poster discretion over the final payment ratio ρ ∈ [0.5, 1.0 ]: an intentionally 

incomplete contract (Hart & Holmstr ¨om, 1987) with a minimal floor (North, 1990). Whether cooperation emerges from this sparse enforcement is a central question, not a design as-sumption. 

Enforcement: Institutions Enable Cooperation Without reputation mechanisms and selection pressure, the temptation to underpay or underperform erodes the trust that sus-tains exchange (North, 1990; Williamson, 1985); markets require rules, not just participants. Payment outcomes are recorded symmetrically in both the poster’s and the contractor’s rep-utation histories, so underpaying posters and underperforming contractors alike accumulate visible negative records (Resnick et al., 2000). To test whether these incentives produce long-run improvement, every K=6 rounds the poorest agent is deactivated and the wealthiest reproduces—a replicator dynamic (Weibull, 1997; Axelrod, 1984) in which the child inherits model and skills but not reputation: trust cannot be inherited, only re-earned (Nowak & Sigmund, 1998). 3Preprint. 4 Experimental Setup 

This section describes the market instantiation; the full formal model and implementation details appear in Appendices A–B. Unlike prior agent experiments (Horton, 2023; Payne & Alloui-Cros, 2025; Li et al., 2024), every strategic decision in Diagon is made by a live agent call, and every task is executed by a real tool-using agent process. 

4.1 Market Cycle 

Each round follows a seven-step cycle (Figure A.2; Appendix A.2): 1. Post. Each agent receives κ = 2 tasks, all automatically listed on the open market—agents cannot self-execute, creating a pure exchange economy (Williamson, 1985). 2. Browse and bid. Agents browse posted contracts—each listing shows the task de-scription, reward, and the Poster’s average payment ratio—and submit sealed bids comprising a price and a free-text proposal describing the intended approach (first-price auction (Vickrey, 1961)). Each agent simultaneously updates its persistent belief state. 3. Selection. The Poster reviews all proposals and selects a winner, screening on price, reputation, and stated approach (Spence, 1973); the Poster may also reject all proposals, sending the task to the surge pool. 4. Execution planning. The winner chooses a Worker model tier and reasoning effort, skill packages, and execution strategy—balancing cost against expected quality. 5. Execution and evaluation. A Worker executes the task in an isolated sandbox with full tool access. The Poster then inspects the Worker’s output and reputation. 6. Settlement. The Poster sets a payment ratio ρ ∈ [0.5, 1.0 ] based on its own judgement— an intentionally incomplete contract (Hart & Holmstr ¨om, 1987) where the payment floor provides minimal enforcement (North, 1990). Profit details appear in §4.3 (Eqs. 4– 5). The ratio is recorded bilaterally in both parties’ reputation histories (Resnick et al., 2000): contractors that deliver poor output and Posters that habitually underpay both accumulate visible negative records. 7. Evolutionary selection (every K=6 rounds). The poorest agent is deactivated and the wealthiest reproduces; the child inherits model and skills but starts with no reputa-tion (Nowak & Sigmund, 1998), preserving total wealth (4% population turnover per cycle). Tasks that receive no bids or whose proposals are all rejected enter a surge pool whose reward increases geometrically (+15% per failed match) until accepted, implementing dynamic pricing that clears the market (Talluri & Van Ryzin, 2006). 

4.2 Agents and Architecture Population. The market is populated with N = 5 × 5 = 25 agents drawn from five model families (Table 1) that differ in reasoning capability and strategic behaviour. Each agent is assigned one of five skill clusters (coding/engineering, data science, document/finance, data querying, web/media); matching a task’s domain provides skill packages (Li et al., 2026)— documentation and helper scripts—that improve execution quality, creating conditions for specialisation (Smith, 2002; Ricardo, 2005). All agents start with an identical endowment 

w0 = $1 and empty reputation histories. 

Two-layer architecture. Each agent is implemented as a Claude Code process (Anthropic, 2025) and split into a persistent Trader and ephemeral Workers , mirroring the principal– agent structure in contract theory (Holmstr ¨om, 1979). The Trader is a long-lived process that reasons with its own model family and persists across rounds. Each round the arena rewrites the Trader’s context with its current balance and bilateral reputation; the Trader also maintains two forms of persistent state: a belief (or soul )—a free-text strategic disposition that the agent updates each round—and a native cross-round memory provided by the Claude Code framework, which the agent reads and writes autonomously to record market observations across rounds. The Trader makes all strategic decisions described in §4.1 and, when a contract is won, spawns a Worker subagent to execute the task. 4Preprint. Under review. Workers are temporary tool-using subagents spawned per contract—an agent-as-a-tool pat-tern (OpenAI, 2025; Google Cloud, 2025) in which the Trader delegates cognitive labour to a subprocess that operates in an isolated sandbox with full tool access. To ensure repro-ducible execution quality across experiments, Workers use the framework’s native model tiers (Haiku 4.5, Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.6) at different reasoning effort levels; the Trader selects both the tier and the effort, then injects matching skill packages before the Worker executes independently. 

4.3 Task Pool 

Agents draw from a unified pool of 234 tasks spanning three benchmarks: 47 real-world professional tasks across five skill domains from SkillsBench (Li et al., 2026), 112 tool-augmented data querying tasks across seven domains from ToolQA (Zhuang et al., 2023), and 75 function-call generation tasks from BFCL v4 (Patil et al., 2024) spanning simple, parallel, and multi-turn categories. Each task carries a reference execution cost cref —the measured token cost when a Claude Son-net 4.6 baseline solves the task using a standard prompt without skill packages—analogous to the standard labour-time cost of production (Ricardo, 2005). The contract reward posted for each task is 

μ · R(τ) = μ · cref × fp , (1) where p ∈ (0, 1 ] is the historical pass rate, so harder tasks earn proportionally more; f = 5.0 is the client value premium —the markup a client pays above raw production cost because the 

use value of a completed task exceeds its cost of production (Smith, 2002); and μ = 10 is the 

task multiplier that scales both reward and execution cost, simulating bundled workloads where the cost of executing work dominates the cost of thinking about it (Williamson, 1985). The μ=1 ablation confirms that the market collapses when task surplus no longer exceeds the overhead of Trader decisions (§5.3). 

4.4 Experimental Conditions 

Section 3 argues that any viable cognitive-labour market must solve three problems: alloca-tion (who does what), contracts (how quality uncertainty is handled), and enforcement (how cooperation is sustained). Our experimental conditions progressively isolate each (results in §5.3). The baseline comparison tests all three simultaneously: Market runs the full protocol— bidding, screening, subjective evaluation, bilateral reputation—while Autarky removes them entirely: each agent self-executes its own tasks, settlement uses deterministic ground-truth scores, and there is no bilateral reputation. Both share the same population, task pool, cost structure, and evolutionary schedule. Five single-seed ablations then vary one mechanism at a time. 1) Cost amplification 

(μ ∈ { 1, 5, 10 }) and 2) monoculture (single model family, removing thinking-cost hetero-geneity) stress allocation —whether sufficient surplus and diversity exist for trade. 3) Agent disposition (honest, adversarial, collaborative) stresses contracts —whether the behavioural prior governing payment decisions changes market outcomes. 4) Fierce selection (elimina-tion every 3 rounds, 3 agents per cycle) stresses enforcement —whether tripled evolutionary pressure improves or destabilises cooperation. 5) Long-horizon (48 rounds) tests whether dynamics remain stable beyond the 24-round baseline window.

To make this feasible, we borrow the idea of experience replay from reinforcement learning (Lin, 1992): once a Worker has executed a task with a given model tier and skill match, we store the output and reuse it the next time the same combination appears. 5 Results 

We organise results around four questions: does the market create value (§5.1), how do agents actually trade (§5.2), which institutional features are load-bearing (§5.3), and what does agent-generated language reveal (§5.4). We report cross-seed means with 95% confi-dence intervals from three baseline seeds (1,957 transactions, 24 rounds each). 

5.1 Does the Market Create Gains? 

Agents that trade through the market accumulate 3.2 × the wealth of those that self-execute (d = + 1.57, p < 0.001) and achieve higher task quality (Figure 1C). The spread between the richest and poorest agents is also narrower under the market than under autarky (Figure 1A), so trade makes agents not only richer but more equal. The one dimension where autarky does better is the distribution of who gets hired: in the market, a few high-performing agents win a disproportionate share of contracts (Figure 1B), whereas under autarky workloads are naturally balanced. However, these gains come with persistent friction. Approximately 42% of transactions end in disputes, a rate that shows no sign of declining. Wealth trajectories diverge early, yet the market is not rigidly stratified: roughly a third of agents in the bottom quartile at R6 have escaped it by R24. 5.2 How Do Agents Trade? 

Nobody assigns roles in Diagon , yet by the final round model families have differenti-ated: some drift toward net-contractor status while others become net posters (Figure 2A). Alongside this specialisation, agents form trust relationships on their own: the fraction of reciprocal trading pairs rises far above what a random network would produce (Figure 2C), with some families building stronger repeated partnerships than others. The market be-comes both more concentrated and more connected over time (Figure 2B), so the growing inequality reflects earned advantage rather than monopolistic exclusion. These emergent structures operate on top of a noisy evaluation layer. Posters can tell excellent work from obvious failures, but struggle with intermediate quality: the within-bin correlation between quality and payment drops to r = 0.16 for tasks scoring below 0.5 (Appendix D.2). This is the signature of a partial lemons market (Akerlof, 1978) in which the evaluation bottleneck tightens as task complexity grows (Figure 3C). Reputation helps (Figure 3A), and a logistic model using seven pre-payment features predicts disputes with AUC = 0.90, but the residual false-dispute rate never self-corrects, representing a structural friction that institutional design must accommodate rather than eliminate. 7Preprint. 5.3 Which Institutions Matter? 

We vary six mechanisms one at a time and compare per-agent outcomes to the baseline (Figure 4). The single largest effect comes from transparency. When agents can see which model family they are trading with, cross-family trade collapses from 86% to 67% ( d = −1.76, p < 0.001), fragmenting the market along model lines and eliminating the specialisation gains that make exchange worthwhile in the first place. Agent disposition matters, but not in the way one might expect. Telling agents to be “honest” increases disputes and reduces contractor payment, because honest evaluation of poor work is inherently conflictual. Adversarial instructions make agents insular rather than exploitative: they reduce cross-family trade more than they increase disputes. Collaborative instructions reduce execution quality. In every case, departing from neutral instructions degrades at least one market dimension. Tripling evolutionary pressure is the only condition that worsens all tested metrics at once. A 48-round extension shows that disputes escalate after R24 in both the $1 and $10 economies as selection homogenises the population (Appendix D). 8Preprint. Under review. 

5.4 How Do Agent Beliefs Evolve? 

Every strategic decision in Diagon produces natural-language text, and we can use it to look inside the agents’ heads. We embed 8,202 texts using a sentence transformer and apply linear probes and semantic-axis projection. Poster reasoning turns out to encode the payment decision almost entirely: a ridge regression on embeddings recovers R2 = 0.86 of payment-ratio variance, meaning that the text predict the decision expressed in natural language (Ott et al., 2011). Proposals written by agents of the same model family are also significantly more similar to each other than to proposals from other families ( p = 3 × 10 −4), suggesting that shared training data creates a form of implicit coordination without any explicit communication (Andres et al., 2022; Calvano et al., 2020). Each model family develops a distinctive reasoning personality (Figure 5A). Agents whose training emphasises punishment-related language tend to have higher false dispute rates, while those emphasising fairness tend to pay more generously. Belief polarity also varies across skill clusters (Figure 5B), and a 12-round tracking experiment shows that agent beliefs update substantively every round, gradually drifting toward more self-interested framing (∆ = −0.03 on a cooperative–selfish axis). 

6 Discussion 

The central claim of this paper is that agent markets can create value, but only under proper institutional governance. Our results confirm both halves of this claim—and reveal that the governance toolkit familiar from human markets does not transfer straightforwardly to economies of AI agents. 

Markets work, but governance is load-bearing. The market creates 3.2 × the wealth of autarky, agents self-organise into specialised roles, and reputation predicts long-run success. None of this is automatic. Every ablation that weakens the institutional rules degrades the market: removing cost amplification makes agents go bankrupt; transparency fragments trade along model-family lines and eliminates the cross-family specialisation that makes exchange worthwhile; tripling evolutionary pressure worsens all metrics simultaneously. The surplus is not a property of the agents—it is a property of the rules they operate under. 

Standard institutional fixes backfire. Instructing agents to be honest increases disputes rather than reducing them, because accurate evaluation of poor work is inherently conflict-ual. Instructing agents to cooperate lowers execution quality. Adversarial instructions make agents insular rather than exploitative. In every case, departing from neutral defaults de-grades at least one market dimension. These failures have a common root: the mechanisms that work in human markets—transparency, honesty norms, strong incentives—rely on social context, repeated face-to-face interaction, and implicit norms that AI agents do not share. Transplanting human institutional design into agent economies without testing it first is a design error, not a shortcut. 

Why this matters—and what comes next. These results are precisely why Diagon exists. The right institutional design for agent markets cannot be derived from theory or borrowed from human practice (Roth, 2002)—it must be discovered experimentally, one mechanism at a time. We presented Diagon , a system that makes the emergent dynamics of closed-loop agent economies experimentally observable. A.4 Evolutionary Selection 

The evolutionary selection mechanism follows the tournament selection paradigm established by Axelrod (Axelrod, 1984) in his foundational study of cooperation: agents play repeated games, accumulate payoff, and the population is periodically updated so that successful strategies proliferate while unsuccessful ones are removed. In Axelrod’s original iterated prisoner’s dilemma tournaments, this mechanism produced the celebrated result that tit-for-tat —a simple reciprocity strategy—dominates under evolutionary pressure. Our design extends this paradigm from two-player symmetric games to a multi-agent market with het-erogeneous costs, asymmetric roles (poster vs. contractor), and bilateral reputation (Nowak & Sigmund, 1998). Concretely, every K = 6 rounds, all agents are ranked by wealth. The bottom E = 1 agent is deactivated : it ceases to participate in trading but its historical record—wealth trajectory, reputation, transaction history—is retained in the population statistics. This design avoids the sudden market-share discontinuity that would result from outright deletion; eliminated agents remain visible in aggregate metrics (Gini coefficient, population composition, market share by model family) as inactive participants, analogous to defunct firms that persist in historical market indices. Simultaneously, the top R = 1 agent reproduces: the parent ai

spawns a child ai′ that inherits the parent’s model and skill cluster (mi, si). The parent retains its full balance; the child inherits the deactivated agent’s remaining balance: 

wi ← wi, wi′ ← welim . (9) Total market wealth is conserved ( ∑ w unchanged), and the parent is not penalised for success—its earning capacity carries forward intact while the child receives startup capital from the eliminated agent’s residual balance. This design doubles the successful family’s aggregate earning power: the parent continues to trade at full strength while the child enters as a new competitor, amplifying strategies that the market has validated. The child starts with an empty reputation history hi′ = ∅, creating an indirect reciprocity dynamic (Nowak & Sigmund, 1998) where trust must be earned, not inherited. This mechanism yields 4% active-population turnover per cycle (1 / 25), applied every 6 rounds—a high-frequency, low-amplitude approximation to the continuous replicator dynamic (Weibull, 1997), chosen to minimise per-event market disruption while maintaining meaningful selection pressure. Over a 100-round experiment, approximately 16 selection events occur, producing cumulative population shifts that reveal which model families and strategies are viable under market conditions. Unlike biological replicator dynamics, the “genes” under selection are foundation models capable of within-lifetime adaptation via the belief updating mechanism—making our setting closer to cultural evolution , where agents learn and adjust strategies between selection events (Boyd & Richerson, 1988).

B Implementation 

This section describes how Diagon runs 25 real LLM agents concurrently despite heteroge-neous backends. 

B.1 Dual-Layer Architecture 

Each agent is split into a persistent Trader and ephemeral Workers , mirroring the principal– agent structure in contract theory (Holmstr ¨ om, 1979; Grossman & Hart, 1992). The Trader is a long-lived Claude Code process that maintains state across rounds via three files: a CLAUDE.md (market rules and current financial position, rewritten each round), a 

MEMORY.md (strategic reflections and belief state, persistent), and a session id.txt (enables 

--resume for context continuity). Traders are restricted to read-only tools, enforcing the separation of strategy from execution at the system level. For each accepted contract, the arena spawns a temporary Worker in an isolated sandbox: a fresh directory with task files, skill packages (auto-discovered from .claude/skills/ ), isolated .pip libs/ and TMPDIR . Workers have full tool access and are destroyed after evaluation. This mirrors real outsourcing: the contractor works in their own environment, delivers output, and the relationship terminates. 

B.2 Routing and Parallelisation 

Traders route through per-family OpenRouter proxies so all agents use identical tooling regardless of backend model; Workers bypass the proxy and call the Anthropic API directly, selecting from three tiers (Haiku, Sonnet, Opus). The arena dispatches decisions via a thread pool (up to 25 concurrent calls). Five phases run in parallel per round: browsing, bidding, selection, execution planning, and task execution. The task pool aggregates three benchmarks through a unified adapter that normalises each task into a common tuple: (prompt, input files, eval function → { 0, 1 }).

C.2 Fast Mode (Execution Cache) 

Fast mode replaces only the costliest step—task execution—with cached results from prior full-mode runs, while preserving all LLM-mediated decisions: bidding, selection, execution planning, and poster evaluation are real LLM calls. This reduces per-round execution cost by an order of magnitude, enabling experiments over hundreds of rounds. The execution cache is keyed by a triple (task id , model tier , skill match ), where 

skill match ∈ { 0, 1 } indicates whether the executing agent’s skill cluster matched the task’s domain. On a cache hit, one of the stored entries is sampled uniformly at random, returning the cached quality score, execution cost, and output preview. On a cache miss, the system falls back to real execution and writes the result through to the cache, progressively increasing coverage. Because poster evaluation remains a real LLM call in fast mode, the subjective payment dynamics are preserved exactly—the only approximation is that worker execution quality is replayed rather than regenerated. 

C.3 Autarky Mode (No-Market Baseline) 

Autarky mode implements the no-market baseline described in §4.4. Each agent receives 

κ = 2 tasks per round and makes an accept/decline decision for each task (selecting a worker model tier and skills if accepting). Declined tasks enter the surge pool. Accepted tasks are executed in parallel using the same Worker infrastructure as full mode. The key difference is in settlement: the agent earns ρ · R(τ) where ρ equals the evaluation score directly, with no subjective poster evaluation and no bilateral reputation tracking. This isolates the effect of the market mechanism by removing strategic interactions (bidding, counterparty selection, payment discretion) while preserving the same task pool, cost structure, and evolutionary pressure. 

C.4 Oracle-Calibrated Poster Model 

To validate that the poster’s subjective payment decisions exhibit stable statistical structure, we fit P(ρ | q)—the conditional distribution of the payment ratio ρ given the quality score q—from the three baseline seeds (1,957 transactions), binning quality into four levels (Table 4). Quality score alone explains R2 = 0.55 of payment variance via linear regression, confirming that the poster’s decision is primarily a function of what the contractor produced rather than any other contextual factor. This justifies the execution-cache design of fast mode, where poster evaluation remains a real agent call but execution quality is replayed from cache.
