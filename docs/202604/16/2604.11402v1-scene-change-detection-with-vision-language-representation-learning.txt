# Abstract 

Scene change detection (SCD) is crucial for urban monitor-ing and navigation but remains challenging in real-world environments due to lighting variations, seasonal shifts, viewpoint differences, and complex urban layouts. Existing methods rely primarily on low-level visual features, limit-ing their ability to accurately identify changed objects amid the visual complexity of urban scenes. In this paper, we propose LangSCD, a vision–language framework for scene change detection that overcomes this single-modal limi-tation by incorporating semantic reasoning through lan-guage. Our approach introduces a modular language com-ponent that leverages vision-language models (VLMs) to generate textual descriptions of scene changes, which are fused with visual features through a cross-modal feature en-hancer. We further introduce a geometric–semantic match-ing module that refines the predicted masks by enforcing se-mantic consistency and spatial completeness. Existing real-world scene change detection benchmarks provide only bi-nary change annotations, which are insufficient for down-stream applications requiring fine-grained understanding of scene dynamics. To address this limitation, we introduce NYC-CD, a large-scale dataset of 8,122 real-world image pairs collected in New York City with multiclass change an-notations generated through a semi-automatic pipeline. Ex-tensive experiments across multiple street-view benchmarks demonstrate that our language and matching modules con-sistently improve existing change-detection architectures, achieving state-of-the-art performance and highlighting the value of integrating linguistic reasoning with visual repre-sentations for robust scene change detection.

# 1. Introduction 

Scene Change Detection (SCD) is a fundamental research challenge with significant industrial applications. It ad-dresses a core question: given two images of the same lo-cation at different times, how can we determine what has changed while distinguishing structural modifications from transient illumination variations (see Fig. 1). High-quality SCD provides the actionable signals necessary for database maintenance, map updating, and Visual Place Recognition (VPR). By identifying these changes, new objects can be in-corporated, removed structures can be pruned, and appear-ance changes can be reflected. Crucially, this change sig-nal should be object–aware (beyond coarse pixel differenc-ing) so that updates target semantically meaningful entities rather than transient noise. Two primary downstream applications depend on high-accuracy SCD: (1) Visual Place Recognition (VPR) : VPR seeks to identify a previously visited location from visual input. However, performance often degrades as the tem-poral gap between query and database images widens, as shown in Fig. 2(a). Seasonal shifts, urban construction, and vegetation growth gradually make static databases ob-solete, causing retrieval recall to collapse. Fig. 2(b) il-lustrates a real-world scene observed over several months, where construction has significantly altered the environ-ment. (2) Long-term Dataset Maintenance : A na¨ ıve maintenance strategy simply appends newly captured im-ages to a database, leading to unbounded growth and slower retrieval. In contrast, SCD enables selective updates by identifying persistent structural modifications rather than transient visual distractors. This results in a more com-pact database, faster retrieval, and improved long-term ro-bustness. Beyond these, SCD enables applications such as anomaly detection [10], mobile robotics [30] and au-tonomous vehicles [14]. Despite significant progress, existing SCD solutions re-main constrained by a single-modal bottleneck: they rely on low-level visual consistency while lacking high-level semantic priors to distinguish task-relevant changes from environmental noise. Simulation-based methods [32] of-ten fail to generalize to real environments, while fixed-viewpoint approaches [46] cannot handle the unconstrained camera motion from mobile platforms. Recent pipelines leveraging vision foundation models [18] produce frag-mented predictions (see Fig. 1), as they struggle to reason about what has changed in a semantically meaningful way. This limitation is particularly acute in urban environ-ments, which we highlight to distinguish from more struc-tured indoor or satellite settings. Urban SCD presents a unique confluence of three challenges: (1) Diverse Change Space: structural alterations—such as construction progress, storefront updates, and billboard changes—co-occur with dynamic traffic and foliage cycles. (2) Un-constrained Acquisition: drastic viewpoint variations in-troduce depth-dependent parallax and occlusions, severely complicating spatial correspondence. (3) High Appearance Volatility: extreme illumination shifts, shadows, and mir-ror reflections create visual “pseudo-changes” that mimic structural updates. These challenges reveal a fundamental gap: existing SCD methods lack explicit, object-level semantic priors that help distinguish meaningful structural changes from nui-sance visual variations. Purely visual pipelines struggle to reason about what changed in complex urban scenes, while directly applying off-the-shelf Vision-Language Models (VLMs) is insufficient. Although VLMs possess strong semantic reasoning capabilities, they often fail to respect the geometric constraints of street-view imagery; under large viewpoint shifts they frequently produce hallucinated regions or coarse localization rather than precise change masks. To address these limitations, we propose LangSCD ,a vision-language framework that tightly couples language-derived semantics with robust visual features. Our key insight is that language can provide structured semantic 2priors describing what changed, while geometric reason-ing ensures spatial consistency across views. Specifically, we introduce a Cross-modal Feature Enhancer that in-jects language-derived semantic cues into visual representa-tions to suppress appearance-driven pseudo-changes, and a 

Geometric–Semantic Matching Module that refines pre-dictions by enforcing spatial completeness and cross-view consistency. Together, these components enable reliable object-level change detection in unconstrained urban envi-ronments. The contributions of this paper are as follows. • We present NYC-CD, a new real-world street-view SCD benchmark. This is the first multi-class SCD benchmark featuring object, appearance, and viewpoint changes in diverse urban environments. To build this bench-mark, we provide a scalable, semi-automatic data annota-tion pipeline that yields high-quality, object-level change masks. • We propose LangSCD, a modular framework for SCD that injects language priors about changes and fuses them with visual features to produce precise object-aware change masks robust to appearance and viewpoint shifts. LangSCD enables robust urban SCD under viewpoint variation, detecting object, appearance, and viewpoint-induced changes jointly. • Strong experimental evidence demonstrates that our plug-and-play modules consistently improve performance across multiple SCD backbones. The gains remain robust under different choices of VLMs including open-source and proprietary models. LangSCD outperforms existing uni-modal or naive VLM-based approaches on both bi-nary and multi-class SCD. 

# 2. Related Works 

2.1. Scene Change Detection Methods 

The majority of existing scene change detection methods share a fundamental limitation: reliance on uni-modal vi-sual information. Supervised learning approaches, whether using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) [1, 11, 33, 43, 46] or vision transformers [12, 26, 38, 45], have achieved notable success in controlled settings. CNN-based meth-ods such as ChangeNet [43] and C-3PO [46] establish strong baselines, while transformer-based methods such as RSCD [26] leverage self-attention mechanisms for im-proved long-range dependency modeling. However, both architectures remain constrained by relying on low-level vi-sual features, which produce fragmented change masks that are disturbed by shadows and reflections while overlooking the semantic context of changed objects. Recent advances in 3D Gaussian splatting [4, 5, 15, 28, 49] and weakly/self-supervised learning [3, 9, 21, 24, 25, 34, 35, 39] address specific challenges but maintain the uni-modal bottleneck. Gaussian splatting methods like 3DGS-CD [28] excel at handling geometric variations through 3D scene representations, yet require dense multiview captures that may not always be available. Weakly supervised ap-proaches such as DiffMatch [24] and self-supervised meth-ods such as EMPLACE [3] reduce annotation requirements but still learn exclusively from visual patterns. Training-free zero-shot methods [17, 18] such as GeSCF offer im-pressive generalization using pre-trained models, but re-main limited by visual-only features. We overcomes this limitation by introducing a language module that integrates seamlessly with existing architectures. By incorporating VLM-generated textual descriptions, our LangSCD consis-tently improves performance across tested baselines, with particularly strong gains on complex urban scenes. Although language-integrated change detection is emerging, existing methods differ significantly from our approach. Remote sensing change detection meth-ods [7, 47] leverage language to detect structured changes with top-down perspective, such as building construction or deforestation, lacking the complexity of street-level vari-ations. Because these changes are structurally simple, they do not require refining its predictions to obtain complete object masks, whereas we explicitly leverage a geometric and semantic matching module to refine initial masks and incorporate object-level information. ViewDelta [44] relies on user-provided text prompts, making the process inefficient. Our method differs by automatically generating descriptions of changes using Vision-Language Models such as GPT [13], then integrating these descriptions with visual features through our cross-modal feature enhancer and verifying them geometrically.

# 3. LangSCD 

3.1. Overall Architecture 

LangSCD integrates language guidance into scene change detection, addressing the fundamental limitations of uni-modal approaches. As illustrated in Figure 3, a standard SCD backbone consists of a shared-weight image encoder, a feature merging module, and a segmentation head; ex-isting methods primarily differ in whether the encoder is CNN-based, DINOv2-based, or SAM-based [18, 26, 46]. We augment this pipeline with two plug-and-play mod-ules. Specifically, the cross-modal feature enhancer takes the encoded feature of Image T1 and enhances it with text features encoding descriptions of new objects in T1. The enhanced T1 feature is then compared with the Image T0

feature using the original backbone’s merging module and segmentation head, producing an initial pixel-level change mask. Building upon this output, the geometric and seman-tic matching modules are applied to refine fragmented ini-tial mask into object-consistent masks by jointly leverag-ing SAM2’s class-agnostic tracking and Grounded SAM’s semantically informed segmentation. This design yields a new end-to-end architecture that integrates semantic rea-soning and geometric consistency in a simple yet effective manner, enabling existing SCD models to address the pre-viously under-explored challenge of street-view semantic change detection. 

3.2. Language Module and Cross-modal Enhancer 

The language module provides semantic guidance to the SCD pipeline through vision–language reasoning. Given an image pair, a Visual-Language Model (VLM) such as GPT-4o, generates a natural language description of objects that appear in T1 but not in T0, and objects with different appear-ances. This description serves as a high-level hypothesis of potential scene changes. The generated text is then encoded by a BERT [6] text backbone, from which we extract the full sequence of token embeddings from the final hidden layer to obtain text features. To integrate language features with visual representa-tions from F1, we employ a cross-modal feature enhancer 

adapted from GroundingDINO [27]. As illustrated in Fig. 3 block 2, Each enhancement layer performs deformable self-attention over image tokens, enabling adaptive focus on relevant regions, while text tokens undergo self-attention to preserve linguistic structure. Cross-modal interaction is achieved through bidirectional per-token cross-attention :• Text-to-image attention allows each spatial image token to attend over all BERT tokens, grounding textual concepts in specific visual regions. • Image-to-text attention enables each text token to refine its embedding using the corresponding visual context, re-inforcing semantic alignment. This cross-modal interaction operates at the token level—each image token attends to all text tokens and vice versa—producing enhanced visual features F ′

that combine spatial localization with semantic cues from the language module.

The geometric matching component leverages SAM2’s tracking capabilities to ensure spatial completeness of de-tected changes [36]. The semantic matching component employs the open vo-cabulary segmentation model Grounded SAM [37] to filter out noise and validate semantic relevance. # 4. Annotation Method 

Our annotation method aims to identify and categorize scene changes between image pairs to create high-quality training data. Given a pair of images T0 and T1 captured at different times from different viewpoints, our method anno-tates three distinct types of change on T1 with respect to T0:(1) new/missing objects, representing objects that appear on 

T1 but not on T0; (2) vegetation appearance changes, cap-turing seasonal alterations in plants; and (3) objects not in view due to viewpoint change, distinguishing geometric oc-clusions from actual scene changes. This multiclass annota-tion approach enables more nuanced understanding of urban scene dynamics compared to binary change detection. Figure 4 presents the pipeline overview. We dive into each module below. 

Change Captioning. The annotation process begins by using GPT-4o to generate descriptions of changes between pairs of images. We provide the model with T0 and T1

along with text prompts for vegetation appearance and ob-ject changes (see supplementary material). In both cases, GPT-4o is explicitly instructed to exclude weather varia-tions, lighting differences, and pedestrians (which are al-ready anonymized in our dataset) to focus on semantically meaningful changes. The model is tasked with identifying objects that appear in T1 but not in T0 as well as vegetation changes. For vegetation appearance changes, we request descriptive attributes such as “green trees” or “bare bushes” to capture seasonal variations. This process yields two lists: changed objects with their descriptions and changed vege-tation with appearance-aware attributes. 

Open Vocabulary Segmentation. The change descrip-tion from GPT-4o are processed through Grounded SAM 2 [37] to produce semantically changed masks. It segments all instances of changed objects in T1, regardless of whether they represent actual changes. For instance, if GPT identi-fies “chairs” as a change due to additional chairs appearing in T1, Grounded SAM 2 segments both preexisting and new chairs. Similarly, vegetation appearance changes are seg-mented using descriptive phrases like “bare trees” or “green foliage,” where the descriptive nature inherently indicates that these segments represent changes. 

SAM2 Tracking. To identify inconsistent objects, we use SAM2 [36] tracking capabilities across the image pair. The process begins with automatic segment generation in 

T1 to produce object segments. These segments are then used as prompts for T0, allowing us to identify segments present in T1 but absent in T0—potential indicators of new objects. However, this raw output often includes irrelevant changes such as shadows or reflections. These geometri-cally inconsistent masks require refinement through match-ing with the semantic masks. 

Object Matching. We refine the change detection by computing the overlap between geometrically inconsistent SAM2 masks and semantically changed G-SAM masks. We retain SAM2 mask if it overlaps with any Grounded SAM2 mask. This matching process leverages the complemen-tary strengths of both approaches: SAM2 tracking iden-tifies changing regions but may include visual distractors, while Grounded SAM 2 identifies semantically relevant ob-jects that are potentially changing regions. The intersec-tion yields high-confidence masks that represent the actual changes in relevant objects. 

Common View Estimation and Matching. To distin-guish between actual scene changes and viewpoint-induced variations, we employ MAST3R [22] for robust dense matching between image pairs. MAST3R excels in two-view regimes, predicting dense correspondences and esti-mating the common-view region that is the area visible in both images. We calculate the overlap between each filtered object mask and the common-view mask. Objects over-lapping with the common view are classified as genuine new/missing object changes, while those outside are cate-gorized as objects not in view. The same classification pro-cess applies to the appearance masks in Grounded SAM 2, distinguishing actual appearance changes from viewpoint-induced occlusions. This step produces our initial pseudo-masks with multi-class labels. 

Manual Inspection. The final stage involves careful human verification of the initial pseudo-masks to ensure annotation quality. We review each image pairs, discard-ing those with false negatives (unlabeled changes in the initial masks) to maintain the completeness of the data set. False positive object masks are manually removed to ensure precision. This semi-automatic approach effi-ciently combines the scalability of automated methods with the accuracy of human judgment, producing high-quality multi-class change detection annotations for complex urban scenes. Figure 5 shows the multi-class change mask an-notated by our semi-automatic method for different image pairs.

To construct meaningful image pairs for change detec-tion, we employ VPR instead of simple GPS matching. VPR retrieves the most visually similar images for a query from a database of images with known camera poses, ensur-ing sufficient visual overlap between the paired images. We specifically use images from different quarters as database and queries (Q1 paired with Q3, Q2 paired with Q4), guar-anteeing a minimum three-month temporal gap between image pairs to capture meaningful scene changes. Using MixVPR [2], a state-of-the-art VPR method, we pair each database image with its top-1 retrieval result to form poten-tial change pairs. This VPR-based approach is crucial be-cause GPS coordinates alone would often yield image pairs with minimal or no viewpoint overlap, making change de-tection infeasible. By prioritizing visual similarity while maintaining temporal separation, we ensure that each poten-tial pair contains sufficient common content for meaningful change analysis while capturing the natural viewpoint vari-ations that occur in real-world scenarios. The NYC-CD dataset introduces several unique charac-teristics that distinguish it from existing benchmarks. Most notably, it explicitly incorporates and annotates viewpoint-induced changes, addressing a critical gap in current datasets that either assume static cameras or attempt to min-imize viewpoint differences. In practical applications like autonomous navigation and augmented reality, viewpoint variations are inevitable and can create apparent changes that are purely geometric rather than semantic. By catego-rizing these as a distinct change type, our dataset enables algorithms to differentiate genuine scene changes from perspective-induced variations. Furthermore, the dataset captures the full complexity of dense urban environments with large temporal spans. The urban environment is rich with diverse changes, including seasonal foliage vari-ations, ongoing construction, evolving storefront appear-ances, and dynamic objects in motion. Unlike controlled simulated environments that cannot replicate real-world un-predictability, NYC-CD provides authentic urban scene dy-namics, making it an essential resource for developing ro-bust change detection systems capable of handling the com-plexities of real-world deployment.

Multi-class supervision. While existing baselines are originally designed for binary change detection, for exper-iments on NYC-CD we extend RSCD to support multi-class change prediction : we modify the RSCD segmenta-tion head to output multiple classes. The modified RSCD is trained using standard pixel-wise cross-entropy loss with the multi-class annotations provided by NYC-CD. 

Evaluation protocol. Our evaluation encompasses four diverse datasets that challenge change detection methods across scenarios and viewpoints. For street-view evalua-tion, we use NYC-CD, VL-CMU-CD [1], PSCD [39], and ChangeVPR [18], which span urban development, seasonal variation, and natural disasters. NYC-CD contains 8,122 image pairs, randomly split into 5,195 training pairs, 1,299 validation pairs, and 1,630 test pairs. For binary evaluation on NYC-CD, all methods are evaluated using binary masks formed as the union of new/missing objects, appearance changes, and viewpoint-induced changes, ensuring fair comparison with binary baselines. For multi-class evaluation, RSCD and RSCD + LangSCD are evaluated, as they explicitly predict multi-class outputs. As noted in prior work [18, 46], the VL-CMU-CD dataset contains samples with clearly inaccurate ground-truth masks. We perform a manual quality check and re-move image pairs with evidently incorrect annotations (e.g., ground-truth masks that completely miss a changed object). Representative examples of noisy labels and the result on the original VL-CMU-CD dataset are shown in the Sup-plementary Material. The same filtered dataset is used to retrain and evaluate all baselines to ensure fair comparison. 

6.2. Comparative studies 

Quantitative comparison. Table 2 reports F1 and IoU results for GeSCF, RSCD, and C-3PO with and with-out LangSCD. Across ChangeVPR , VL-CMU-CD , and 

PSCD , our approach consistently improves all baselines. On ChangeVPR, which contains strong appearance varia-tions typical in long-term VPR, the gains are particularly large: GeSCF improves from 0.47 to 0.64 F1 (+17%), RSCD from 0.24 to 0.63 (+39%), and C-3PO from 0.12 to 0.58 (+46%), with similar improvements in IoU. On VL-CMU-CD and PSCD, the improvements are more mod-erate but remain consistent across architectures. For ex-ample, RSCD+LangSCD achieves the best performance on VL-CMU-CD with 0.85 F1 and 0.75 IoU, while C-3PO+LangSCD achieves the best results on PSCD with 80.70 F1 and 0.58 IoU. These results indicate that language-guided reasoning provides semantic cues, while object-complete matching improves spatial consistency, jointly benefiting different SCD architectures across datasets. The largest improvements appear on NYC-CD , our pro-posed benchmark. This dataset is significantly more chal-lenging due to frequent appearance changes, complex ur-ban scenes, and strong viewpoint variations that often de-grade purely visual methods. Consequently, baseline per-formance is relatively low. By incorporating language-guided semantic reasoning and geometric matching, our LangSCD improves detection accuracy across all baselines. RSCD+LangSCD achieves the best results with 0.70 F1 and 0.58 IoU, corresponding to improvements of +57% and +41%, respectively. Similar gains are observed for GeSCF and C-3PO, highlighting the effectiveness of language guid-ance in challenging urban environments where distinguish-ing true structural changes from distracting visual variations requires semantic reasoning. Multi-class change detection analysis in Table 3 corrobo-rates our approach. The RSCD baseline shows varying per-formance across categories, with dynamic changes proving more challenging. Integrating our modules improves results across all classes, with particularly strong gains in the most difficult categories such as object not in view (refer to the supplementary material for qualitative comparison). Over-all, these enhancements yield higher mean performance and demonstrate more balanced and robust detection, especially in scenarios involving geometric transformations and ap-pearance variations. 

Qualitative comparison. In Fig. 1, our method pro-duces accurate, object-complete change masks across di-verse urban scenes, clearly distinguishing three types of semantic changes: object changes, vegetation appearance changes, and viewpoint-induced changes. Compared with the SOTA RSCD model, which predicts only binary change masks and often yields fragmented detections or misses meaningful changes, our predictions align closely with ob-ject boundaries and maintain spatial completeness. The re-sults show that adding LangSCD helps localize structural changes such as new or missing objects, capture vegeta-tion appearance variations, and correctly identify regions affected by viewpoint shifts. This qualitative comparison highlights the advantage of language-guided reasoning and object-complete matching in producing semantically mean-ingful and geometrically consistent change masks. 

Latency. Since captions are generated online at infer-ence, the runtime is dominated by the language compo-nent. The complete pipeline processes each image pair in an average of 4.89 seconds, with the majority of time at-tributed to caption generation, while the visual forward pass and geometric–semantic refinement contribute only a small fraction of the total cost (refer to the supplementary mate-rial). Our goal is not real-time perception but enabling a new capability for street-view scene change detection un-der large viewpoint differences, a challenging setting where existing visual-only methods often fail. In practice, such change detection is primarily used for offline tasks such as updating VPR databases or maintaining long-term maps, where images are processed asynchronously rather than in real time. In these scenarios, reliable change identification is more critical than per-frame speed. Improving inference efficiency, for example, through lightweight language mod-els, caching, or selective invocation of the language module, remains an important direction for future work.

In this work, we present LangSCD , a framework that enables robust urban scene change detection in uncon-strained street-view environments. By injecting semantic priors through a cross-modal feature enhancer and enforcing object-complete consistency with geometric-semantic matching, our approach resolves distractors and viewpoint-induced ambiguities that challenge existing visual-only methods. # 1. NYC-CD Dataset Curation Statistics 

The initial candidate pool contained 9,000 image pairs. During the final manual verification stage, 878 pairs were discarded due to incomplete pseudo-masks (e.g., missing change objects) or severe annotation ambiguity, resulting in a final dataset of 8,122 image pairs. We further ana-lyze the distribution of change categories across the dataset. Among the curated pairs, 5,040 pairs contain new or miss-ing object changes, 5,093 pairs contain vegetation appear-ance changes, and 7,201 pairs exhibit viewpoint-induced changes caused by camera repositioning and parallax ef-fects. Note that these categories are not mutually exclusive; a single image pair may contain multiple types of changes simultaneously. The high proportion of viewpoint changes reflects the unconstrained acquisition conditions of street-view imagery, where camera pose differences are common across temporal captures.

# 3. Training Protocol 

This section provides detailed information on the training configuration, including which components are trainable, dataset usage, and optimization settings. 

Trainable and Frozen Components. LangSCD is de-signed as a lightweight extension to existing scene change detection (SCD) backbones. During training, the Cross-modal Feature Enhancer , Merge Module , and Convo-lution & Upsampling segmentation head are trainable. All other components are frozen, including the Multimodal LLM used for change captioning, the Text Feature Back-bone (BERT), the Image Encoder backbone (e.g., DI-NOv2 or CNN encoders depending on the baseline), and the Geometric–Semantic Matching module , which relies on pretrained SAM2 and Grounded SAM models. 

Dataset-specific Training. For fair comparison, models are trained separately on each dataset used for evaluation rather than performing cross-dataset transfer. For example, when evaluating on VL-CMU-CD, PSCD, ChangeVPR, or NYC-CD, the corresponding training split of that dataset is used for training and the validation split is used for model selection. This protocol avoids cross-dataset leakage and ensures consistency with the training procedures adopted in prior work. 

Optimization and Hyperparameters. Training follows the configuration of the underlying SCD backbone with minimal modification. For RSCD-based experiments, the DINOv2 encoder is kept frozen while the remaining train-able modules are optimized. Models are trained for 100 epochs using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 1 × 10 −4. A cosine learning-rate scheduler is used together with a warm-up stage at the beginning of training. The batch size is 4 and training uses mixed precision to improve efficiency. Random horizontal flipping with prob-ability 0.5 is used for data augmentation. Input resolution follows the dataset protocol (e.g., 504 × 504 for VL-CMU-CD ). For C-3PO experiments, we adopt the same optimization strategy while using a VGG16-based DeepLabV3 architec-ture with an input resolution of 640 × 640 .

Loss Function. Binary scene change detection is opti-mized using pixel-wise cross-entropy loss . To address foreground–background imbalance, fixed class weights of 

[0.025, 0.975] are applied during training to emphasize changed regions. 

Threshold Selection. The thresholds used in the geomet-ric and semantic matching modules are selected using the 

NYC-CD validation set . A threshold sweep is performed to determine the operating point, resulting in αt = 0 .20 

for geometric matching and αg = 0 .10 for semantic match-ing. These thresholds are then fixed across all datasets and backbones without further tuning.

# 6. Evaluation of GPT-Generated Change Cap-tions 

To assess the reliability of GPT in describing object-level scene changes, we conducted a human evaluation on a ran-domly selected subset of 800 image pairs from our dataset. For each pair, GPT produced a change caption listing new objects present in image I0 but absent in image I1. We eval-uated these captions along two criteria: 1. Completeness – whether all truly changed (new or miss-ing) objects were included. 2. Precision – whether the caption avoided listing objects that did not undergo change. Each caption was manually verified by annotators with access to the ground-truth change masks. Errors were recorded at the object level: • a false positive when GPT listed an object that was not actually new, and • a false negative when GPT failed to list a genuine new/missing object. Across the 800 evaluated image pairs, GPT produced a total of 75 false-positive objects and 37 false-negative ob-jects . Although GPT generally identifies the major scene differences, these results show that caption-only change de-tection may miss subtle changes and may hallucinate ob-jects in visually ambiguous regions. This analysis moti-vates the need for the structured language-guided modules described in the main paper, which leverage captions while enforcing consistency with visual evidence.
