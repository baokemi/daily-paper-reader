# Predictive Entropy Links Calibration and Paraphrase Sensitivity in Medical Vision-Language Models 

Binesh Sadanadan 1 Vahid Behzadan 11SAIL Lab, University of New Haven, West Haven, CT, USA 

Abstract 

Medical Vision-Language Models (VLMs) suf-fer from two failure modes that threaten safe deployment: miscalibrated confidence and sen-sitivity to question rephrasing. We show they share a common cause, proximity to the de-cision boundary, by benchmarking five uncer-tainty quantification methods on MedGemma-4B-IT across in-distribution (MIMIC-CXR) and out-of-distribution (PadChest) chest X-ray datasets, with cross-architecture validation on LLaVA-RAD-7B. For well-calibrated single-model methods, pre-dictive entropy from one forward pass predicts which samples will flip under rephrasing (AU-ROC = 0.711 on MedGemma, 0.878 on LLaVA-RAD; p < 10 −4), enabling a single entropy thresh-old to flag both unreliable and rephrase-sensitive predictions. A five-member LoRA ensemble fails under the MIMIC →PadChest shift (42.9% ECE, 34.1% accuracy), though LLaVA-RAD’s ensemble does not collapse (69.1%). MC Dropout achieves the best calibration (ECE = 4.3%) and selective prediction coverage (21.5% at 5% risk), yet total entropy from a single forward pass outperforms the ensemble for both error detection (AUROC = 0.743 vs. 0.657) and paraphrase screening. Simple meth-ods win. 

1 INTRODUCTION 

A radiologist asks a Vision-Language Model (VLM) whether a chest X-ray shows pleural effusion. The model answers “Yes” with 94% softmax confidence. The radiolo-gist moves on. But the model is wrong, and its confidence provides no warning. Worse, if the radiologist asks the same question in slightly different words, the model might flip its answer to “No.” Miscalibrated confidence in medical AI isn’t a technical nuisance; it’s a patient safety problem. When clinicians can’t distinguish reliable predictions from overconfident errors, they lose the ability to exercise ap-propriate skepticism, request second opinions, or escalate ambiguous cases [Kompa et al., 2021, Jiang et al., 2012]. The promise of medical VLMs for clinical decision sup-port [Google Health AI, 2025, Moor et al., 2023, Li et al., 2024] therefore depends on a capability that current evalua-tions largely ignore: well-calibrated uncertainty. Three compounding problems make Uncertainty Quantifica-tion (UQ) especially urgent for medical VLMs. First, stan-dard softmax probabilities are poorly calibrated; modern deep networks tend to be overconfident [Guo et al., 2017], and this miscalibration worsens with scale and domain-specific fine-tuning. Second, clinical deployment inevitably involves distribution shift: new scanner hardware, different patient demographics, or degraded acquisition quality. Cali-bration that looks adequate in-distribution can deteriorate under even mild corruption [Ovadia et al., 2019]. Third, medical VLMs have a separate fragility that calibration alone doesn’t address: they often give contradictory answers when a question is rephrased, even if the meaning hasn’t changed [Anonymous, 2026b]. Prior work treats miscali-bration and paraphrase sensitivity as independent failure modes. We argue they are symptoms of the same underlying problem. Despite growing interest in medical VLMs, existing evalu-ations focus almost exclusively on accuracy. Benchmarks like VQA-RAD [Lau et al., 2018] and SLAKE [Liu et al., 2021] report accuracy and F1 but not whether a model’s confidence tracks its actual correctness. Recent robustness evaluations confirm this gap: VLMs degrade under com-mon imaging artifacts, yet no study measures whether their uncertainty scores remain reliable under these shifts [Kahl et al., 2024]. Meanwhile, the UQ literature has developed mature methods: temperature scaling [Guo et al., 2017], MC Dropout [Gal and Ghahramani, 2016], deep ensem-bles [Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017], and conformal pre-diction [Angelopoulos and Bates, 2021]. Recent work has

proposed Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) ensembles as a parameter-efficient route to calibrated uncertainty in large language models [Wang and Aitchison, 2023, Mühlemat-ter et al., 2026]. These studies report improved calibration in-distribution, but deep ensembles perform best on obvi-ous far-distribution shifts and can fail on more subtle near-distribution data [Vasiliuk et al., 2023, Seligmann et al., 2023]. No prior work has tested LoRA ensembles under the cross-site shifts that define real clinical deployment, where the distribution change is subtle enough to evade detection but large enough to break calibration. We evaluate five UQ methods on MedGemma-4B-IT [Google Health AI, 2025] across in-distribution (MIMIC-CXR) and out-of-distribution (PadChest) chest X-ray datasets, with cross-architecture validation on LLaVA-RAD-7B [Chaves et al., 2024]. We discover that predictive entropy serves as a unified proxy for both error detection and para-phrase vulnerability. This paper makes four contributions: 1. Uncertainty predicts paraphrase sensitivity. For well-calibrated single-model methods, predictions with high predictive entropy are significantly more likely to flip under rephrasing (area under the ROC curve, AUROC = 0.711; p < 10 −26 ). Paraphrase instability is not a separate failure mode; it’s a symptom of decision-boundary proximity that entropy already captures. A single entropy threshold can flag both unreliable and rephrase-unstable predictions, giving clinicians one abstention criterion instead of two. 2. LoRA ensembles fail out-of-distribution. A five-member LoRA ensemble [Hu et al., 2022] per-forms well in-distribution but fails under the MIMIC →PadChest shift, producing an Expected Cal-ibration Error (ECE) of 42.9% and accuracy of just 34.1%. Per-member analysis reveals that only one of five adapters generalizes; probability averaging lets four poorly performing members outvote the one that works. This failure inverts the uncertainty-paraphrase bridge signal (AUROC = 0.377). 3. Entropy decomposition is method-dependent. To-tal predictive entropy detects errors well (AU-ROC = 0.743). MC Dropout at standard dropout rates yields negligible epistemic uncertainty, while the deep ensemble produces meaningful mutual information (0.082 nats), elevated for errors [Depeweg et al., 2018, Smith and Gal, 2018]. Yet total entropy from a single model still outperforms. 4. Selective prediction under distribution shift. MC Dropout covers 21.5% of samples at 5% target risk on out-of-distribution data versus 7.3% for softmax. The deep ensemble achieves zero coverage at this threshold. Together, these results point to a practical recommendation: a well-fine-tuned single model with softmax entropy han-dles calibration, error detection, and paraphrase sensitivity screening in one forward pass. Cross-architecture valida-tion on LLaVA-RAD-7B confirms the bridge generalizes (AUROC = 0.878 on MIMIC), supporting the geometric explanation: near-boundary predictions have higher entropy and flip more, regardless of architecture. LoRA ensembles trained on single-site data should not be deployed cross-site without per-member validation. 

2 RELATED WORK 

Calibration in deep learning. Guo et al. [2017] showed that modern neural networks are poorly calibrated: confi-dence scores systematically overestimate correctness. Tem-perature scaling [Platt, 1999] remains the dominant post-hoc fix, and ECE [Naeini et al., 2015] the standard met-ric, though binning artifacts have prompted adaptive vari-ants [Nixon et al., 2019]. Calibration degrades under distri-bution shift [Ovadia et al., 2019], a routine setting in clinical deployment where patient populations, imaging hardware, and protocols vary across sites. 

Bayesian and ensemble uncertainty. MC Dropout [Gal and Ghahramani, 2016] reinterprets test-time dropout as approximate variational inference, yielding epistemic uncer-tainty at low cost. Deep ensembles [Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017] consistently outperform single models on calibration and out-of-distribution detection. Decomposing predictive uncertainty into epistemic and aleatoric components [De-peweg et al., 2018] informs safe deployment: epistemic uncertainty should trigger abstention, while aleatoric un-certainty reflects irreducible noise. Mutual information pro-vides a principled epistemic estimator [Smith and Gal, 2018, Houlsby et al., 2011]. Recent work extends this to parameter-efficient settings: Wang and Aitchison [2023] propose LoRA ensembles for LLM calibration, Mühlematter et al. [2026] show they can match explicit ensembles on vision tasks, and Yang et al. [2024] apply Laplace approximation to LoRA parameters for calibrated posteriors without multiple for-ward passes. None of these evaluate cross-site medical de-ployment. Our bridge finding depends on total predictive entropy and is agnostic to which UQ method produces it, so BayesLoRA-style posteriors could serve as an alterna-tive source; we leave this comparison to future work. More broadly, Seligmann et al. [2023] identify failure modes for ensembles of fine-tuned transformers under distribution shift in a large-scale evaluation on WILDS benchmarks. 

Uncertainty in medical AI. Prior work has studied UQ for medical image classification, including skin lesion diag-nosis [Combalia et al., 2020], retinal disease grading [Leibig et al., 2017], and chest X-ray pathology detection [Ghesu et al., 2021]. These efforts address unimodal, image-only classifiers. For OOD detection specifically, Vasiliuk et al. [2023] show that ensembles and MC Dropout fail on subtle near-distribution shifts in 3D medical segmentation, even when they succeed on obvious far-distribution data. Medical VLMs [Li et al., 2024, Chen et al., 2024, Zhang et al., 2024, Google Health AI, 2025] introduce text-conditioned reason-ing, yet evaluation stays focused on accuracy benchmarks. The CARES benchmark [Xia et al., 2024] measures trust-worthiness dimensions like fairness and safety but omits calibration. No prior study systematically evaluates calibra-tion or UQ for medical VLMs. 

Selective prediction. Selective prediction lets a model abstain on low-confidence inputs, trading coverage for accuracy [El-Yaniv and Wiener, 2010, Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2017]. The Area Under the Risk-Coverage Curve (AURC) [Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2019] summarizes this trade-off. Conformal prediction [Vovk et al., 2005, An-gelopoulos and Bates, 2021] and learn-then-test frame-works [Angelopoulos and Bates, 2022] provide finite-sample risk guarantees. Lu et al. [2022] extended conformal methods for equitable coverage across demographic sub-groups. 

Paraphrase sensitivity. The PSF-Med bench-mark [Anonymous, 2026b] showed that medical VLMs produce contradictory answers to paraphrased questions at high rates. Elazar et al. [2021] and Ribeiro et al. [2020] established that such linguistic sensitivity failures are pervasive across language models. No prior work has tested whether paraphrase sensitivity and poor calibration share a common mechanism. 

Our contribution. Prior work treats calibration, selective prediction, and paraphrase sensitivity as separate problems. To our knowledge, no study has (i) systematically evaluated UQ quality for medical VLMs, (ii) formally connected para-phrase sensitivity to predictive uncertainty, or (iii) shown that a single entropy threshold can flag both unreliable and rephrase-unstable predictions.

4 METHODS 

4.1 UNCERTAINTY ESTIMATION METHODS 

We evaluate five UQ methods that span single-pass, post-hoc, and ensemble-based approaches. All methods operate on the decision margin m(x, q ) = ℓYes − ℓNo and softmax probability ˆp = σ(m) defined in Section 3.1. 

Softmax entropy (baseline). A single forward pass yields the predictive entropy H[ˆ p] (Eq. 3) and confidence c =max(ˆ p, 1 − ˆp). This method adds no computation beyond standard inference. 

Temperature scaling. We learn a scalar T > 0 on a held-out calibration partition (15% of evaluation data) by minimizing negative log-likelihood via L-BFGS [Guo et al., 2017]. At test time, ˆpT = σ(m/T ∗). Temperature scaling preserves the decision boundary while reshaping the con-fidence distribution; T ∗ > 1 corrects the overconfidence typical of large language models [Kadavath et al., 2022]. All reported metrics come from the remaining 85%. 

MC Dropout. Our LoRA adapters include dropout (pdrop = 0 .05 ) on each low-rank projection. At test time we keep dropout active and run K = 10 stochastic forward passes, producing probability samples {p(k)}Kk=1 [Gal and Ghahramani, 2016]. The predictive mean ¯p = 1

Deep ensemble. We train M = 5 LoRA adapters inde-pendently with different random seeds [Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017]. We average ensemble predictions in probability space and apply the same entropy decomposition as MC Dropout. Because each adapter follows a different optimiza-tion trajectory, the ensemble captures functional diversity that MC Dropout cannot [Fort et al., 2019]. 

Margin magnitude. The absolute decision margin 

|m(x, q )| serves as a non-parametric confidence score. Large margins mean the “Yes” and “No” logits sit far apart; small margins mean near-equipoise. Because margin derives 

ˆp from the same forward pass as softmax, their calibration metrics (ECE, Brier, NLL) are identical by construction; the methods differ only in the ranking used for selective prediction.

Geometric intuition. The connection rests on a simple observation. When the decision margin m is large, the “Yes” and “No” logits sit far apart; rephrasing shifts the internal representation slightly, but not enough to cross the boundary. When m ≈ 0, even a small perturbation can flip the answer. High predictive entropy signals exactly this near-boundary regime. If this reasoning holds, uncertainty scores should be systematically higher for flip-prone predictions than for stable ones. 

Testing the bridge. We test this with two analyses. First, a group comparison: we split the test set into flipped ( fi = 1 )and stable ( fi = 0 ) subsets and compare entropy distribu-tions via the Mann-Whitney U test. Second, a discrimination test: we treat flip prediction as binary classification and re-port the AUROC of each method’s uncertainty score as the positive-class predictor. If both confirm the hypothesis, para-phrase sensitivity can be recast as a symptom of predictive uncertainty rather than an independent failure mode, and a single entropy threshold can serve as a unified abstention criterion.

The base MedGemma model is poorly calibrated: ECE of 44.1%, Brier score 0.429, and NLL 1.311, confirming that raw soft-max probabilities from medical VLMs are unsuitable for clinical decision support. 5.4 ENTROPY DECOMPOSITION 

Table 6 reports the entropy decomposition on PadChest. The central finding is method-dependent: the deep ensemble pro-duces meaningful epistemic MI while MC Dropout does not. MC-D ROP yields MI of just 0.0002 nats (MI/H ≈ 0), because LoRA dropout at pdrop = 0 .05 perturbs too little of the 4.38M-parameter adapter to produce meaningful varia-tion. 6 DISCUSSION 

The bridge between uncertainty and paraphrase sen-sitivity. The connection between predictive entropy and paraphrase sensitivity is our central finding. On MedGemma-4B-IT under the MIMIC →PadChest shift, flip-prone predic-tions carry higher entropy across all single-model methods (AUROC = 0.711, p < 10 −26 ), and the same pattern holds on LLaVA-RAD-7B (AUROC = 0.878 on MIMIC, 0.706 on PadChest). The explanation is geometric: predictions near the decision boundary produce elevated entropy and flip more under rephrasing. Consistency training [Anony-mous, 2026b] reduces flip rates by sharpening entropy dis-tributions, suggesting these are two views of the same phe-nomenon. A deployment system gets paraphrase sensitivity screening for free alongside uncertainty estimation. 

Why LoRA ensembles fail out-of-distribution. The MedGemma ensemble’s OOD failure runs counter to the standard finding that deep ensembles provide strong uncer-tainty under shift [Ovadia et al., 2019, Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017], though recent evaluations document similar failures for fine-tuned transformers [Seligmann et al., 2023]. The LLaVA-RAD ensemble does not exhibit this failure (69.1% accuracy vs. MedGemma’s 34.1%), suggesting the mechanism is model-specific. For MedGemma, only seed 42 generalizes; probability averaging lets four poorly perform-ing members dilute its signal (Appendix G). The ensemble produces meaningful MI (0.082 nats, elevated for errors), but this diversity does not improve error detection because OOD accuracy is too poor. Neither logit averaging nor ma-jority vote rescues performance (Appendix G). 

Clinical deployment recommendations. Our results on MedGemma-4B-IT under the MIMIC →PadChest shift point toward a two-tier deployment protocol, summarized in Table 10. Table 10: Recommended deployment tiers. Tier selection depends on whether latency or risk control is the binding constraint.

Tier 1 uses a single forward pass with softmax entropy; Tier 2 uses MC Dropout ( K=10 ) for stricter risk control at 10 × inference cost. LoRA ensembles trained on single-site data should not be deployed cross-site without per-member OOD validation. Entropy thresholds should be recalibrated on local validation data (Appendix I). The full inference protocol appears in Appendix M. 

Robustness under image corruption. Targeted LoRA calibration is remarkably stable under synthetic corruption (Table 4). AUGRC improves from 0.091 (clean) to 0.084 at severity 5, because corruption pushes the model toward con-servative predictions that reduce undetected failures. Con-formal coverage guarantees also hold: calibrating on clean data and testing on severity-5 corruptions produces over-coverage (92.4% empirical vs. 90% target; Appendix J.1). By contrast, Base model AUGRC degrades by +0.020 and conformal coverage drops by 7.5 percentage points. This supports deploying Targeted LoRA in settings where image quality varies across clinical sites. 

Limitations. Both models share the single cross-site tran-sition (MIMIC-CXR →PadChest); other site pairs may show different calibration properties. We evaluate binary yes/no questions only; free-text generation requires semantic en-tropy [Kuhn et al., 2023]. The geometric bridge argument should generalize to meaning-space boundaries but we have not tested this. The ensemble failure is model-specific (LLaVA-RAD main-tains 69.1% OOD accuracy). MC Dropout’s negligible MI extends to pdrop = 0 .20 (Appendix H), suggesting the bot-tleneck is adapter-only scope (4.38M/4B parameters). Our entropy threshold can be viewed as a simple routing policy; more sophisticated routing to stronger models or retrieval-augmented pipelines is a natural extension. The MIMIC-CXR subset ( n = 98 ) limits standalone statistical claims, which we mitigate through bootstrap CIs. Our synthetic cor-ruptions approximate but do not capture the full complexity of real clinical distribution shifts. Future directions appear in Appendix O.

C IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS 

C.1 MODEL SPECIFICATION 

All experiments use MedGemma-4B-IT [Google Health AI, 2025] ( google/medgemma-4b-it ), a medical VLM based on the Gemma 2 architecture with 34 transformer layers and a SigLIP vision encoder producing 256 image tokens. The model processes chest radiographs alongside a text prompt and generates binary yes/no answers. We extract logits for the “Yes” and “No” tokens (including space-prefixed variants) at the first generated token position.

E.5 AUROC FOR FLIP PREDICTION 

To evaluate how well uncertainty predicts paraphrase sen-sitivity as a binary classification task, we compute the AU-ROC using the predictive entropy of each sample as the score and the flip/stable label as the binary outcome. The AUROC equals the probability that a randomly chosen flipped prediction has higher entropy than a randomly cho-sen stable prediction, providing a threshold-free measure of discriminative ability.

O FUTURE DIRECTIONS 

Five directions extend this work. 

Higher MC Dropout rates. Dropout rates of 0.1–0.2 may produce meaningful epistemic decomposition with LoRA-adapted medical VLMs. The key question is at what cost to base accuracy, since the adapters were trained with pdrop =0.05 . Retraining with matched dropout rates would clarify this trade-off. 

Diverse ensemble training. Each adapter in our ensemble sees the same training data. Training each on a different data subset, with a different LoRA rank, or targeting different layers could prevent the shared-bias failure we observe. This tests whether diversity in the training procedure prevents the OOD failure rather than simply hoping that random seed variation provides sufficient functional diversity. 

Semantic entropy for free-text VQA. Our binary re-duction enables clean uncertainty extraction but excludes open-ended generation. Free-text output requires aggregat-ing token-level uncertainties into meaning-level estimates. Semantic entropy [Kuhn et al., 2023] clusters generations by meaning and computes entropy over clusters, extending the UQ-PSF bridge to settings where paraphrase sensitivity manifests as semantic drift rather than binary flips. 

Conformal prediction with conditional guarantees. Ap-pendix J provides a preliminary conformal baseline. Distribution-free coverage guarantees [Angelopoulos and Bates, 2021] complement the heuristic thresholds in our selective prediction analysis. Conditional extensions that ensure guarantees hold across clinical subgroups [Lu et al., 2022] (e.g., by disease prevalence, patient demographics, or imaging site) would strengthen the fairness properties of the abstention criterion. 

Real-world distribution shift validation. Our five syn-thetic corruption types approximate but do not capture the full complexity of clinical distribution shifts. Validation on multi-site clinical data with natural shifts across scanner manufacturers, imaging protocols, and patient demograph-ics would test whether the calibration stability we observe transfers to deployment. Multi-center studies with prospec-tive data collection would provide the strongest evidence.

Training. We train five targeted LoRA adapters on LLaVA-RAD’s Vicuna-7B backbone (layers 14–18 of 32, proportionally matching MedGemma’s 15–19 of 34) using the same hyperparameters ( r=16 , α=32 , pdrop =0 .05 ) and combined consistency-accuracy loss. Key differences from MedGemma. (1) LLaVA-RAD’s base model shows extreme yes-bias on PadChest (99.3% positive predictions), yielding only 11 flips and making the base bridge AUROC unreliable. LoRA corrects this bias. (2) LLaVA-RAD’s ensemble does not collapse on PadChest (69.1% accuracy vs. MedGemma’s 34.1%), suggesting the MedGemma ensemble failure is model-specific. (3) MC Dropout produces negligible epistemic uncertainty on both architectures, confirming this is an inherent limitation of adapter-only dropout.

Four themes run through these results. First, LoRA fine-tuning is the primary calibration mechanism; no post-hoc UQ method applied to the base model comes close. Second, deep ensembles of LoRA adapters can fail OOD, but the fail-ure is model-specific: MedGemma’s ensemble collapses on PadChest (34.1% accuracy) while LLaVA-RAD’s maintains 69.1%. Third, simple UQ methods suffice. Softmax entropy from a single forward pass achieves the best flip-prediction AUROC (0.711 on MedGemma, 0.706 on LLaVA-RAD for PadChest OOD) and the best error-detection AUROC (0.743). MC Dropout adds value for selective prediction at strict risk thresholds (21.5% vs. 7.3% coverage at 5% risk). Fourth, the UQ-paraphrase bridge generalizes across architectures: the geometric argument that near-boundary predictions produce both high entropy and paraphrase vul-nerability holds on fundamentally different model families. The cost-effective recommendation: a well-fine-tuned sin-gle model with softmax entropy, reserving MC Dropout for scenarios that require strict selective prediction guarantees.
